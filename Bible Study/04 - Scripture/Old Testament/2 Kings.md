# 2 Kings

**Overview:** The slow, painful decline and fall of both the northern and southern kingdoms. Israel (north) falls to Assyria in 722 BC; Judah survives longer but is finally destroyed by Babylon in 586 BC — the Temple burned, Jerusalem leveled, the people exiled. Occasional good kings delay but cannot reverse the trajectory set by persistent idolatry and covenant breaking.

---

### Key Moments

- **Elijah taken up in the whirlwind (2 Kings 2):** Elijah departs in a chariot of fire; Elisha watches and cries out *"My father, my father! The chariots of Israel and its horsemen!"* He takes up Elijah's mantle and the spirit rests on him doubly.

- **Elisha receives a double portion (2 Kings 2):** Elisha asks for twice Elijah's spirit. His subsequent miracles — more numerous than Elijah's — confirm it was granted. The torch of prophetic ministry passes.

- **The widow's oil, the Shunammite's son, Naaman healed (2 Kings 4–5):** Elisha multiplies a widow's oil to save her from debt slavery; raises the Shunammite's son from death; and heals Naaman, a Syrian military commander, of leprosy — through the humbling instruction to dip seven times in the Jordan. Naaman's healing crosses ethnic and national lines.

- **Israel falls to Assyria; ten tribes exiled (2 Kings 17):** The northern kingdom is conquered by Assyria. The text pauses to deliver a theological verdict: this happened because Israel rejected God's covenant, worshipped other gods, and ignored every prophet sent to warn them. The northern tribes are scattered and lost to history.

- **Hezekiah's prayer and the Assyrian threat (2 Kings 18–19):** Sennacherib's field commander mocks Hezekiah and YHWH before the walls of Jerusalem. Hezekiah spreads the threatening letter before God in the Temple and prays. Isaiah prophesies deliverance. That night, 185,000 Assyrian soldiers die. Jerusalem is spared.

- **Isaiah's prophecy about Babylon (2 Kings 20):** After Hezekiah foolishly shows Babylon's envoys all his treasury, Isaiah prophesies that everything — including his sons — will be taken to Babylon. The shadow of the next empire falls across the narrative.

- **Josiah's reformation — the Book of the Law found (2 Kings 22–23):** During Temple repairs, the Book of the Law is discovered. When Hezekiah hears it read he tears his robes. The prophetess Huldah confirms judgment is coming — but not in Josiah's lifetime because of his responsive heart. Josiah leads the most sweeping reformation in Judah's history: high places demolished, Asherah poles burned, the Passover kept for the first time since the judges.

- **Nebuchadnezzar's sieges; Temple destroyed; Jerusalem burned (2 Kings 24–25):** Three sieges progressively strip the city. In 586 BC the Temple is plundered and burned, Jerusalem's walls torn down, the leading citizens killed or deported to Babylon. The last king, Zedekiah, watches his sons executed, then has his eyes put out. The book ends with the faintest note of grace: the exiled king Jehoiachin is released from prison and given a place at the Babylonian king's table.

---

### Main Characters

**Elijah** — the great prophet who appears briefly at the start; his fiery departure marks the transition to Elisha. **Elisha** — Elijah's successor, whose miracles span the first half of the book; a figure of compassion, power, and occasional severity. **Naaman** — Syrian military commander healed of leprosy; his story is one of the most theologically suggestive in the OT, foreshadowing grace to outsiders. **Hezekiah** — Judah's most faithful king after David; his prayer saves Jerusalem from Assyria, though his later pride opens the door to Babylon. **Josiah** — the last great reforming king; his discovery of the Torah and sweeping repentance is genuine but comes too late to avert exile. **Zedekiah** — Judah's last king; a puppet of circumstance who ignores Jeremiah's counsel and presides over the final catastrophe. **Nebuchadnezzar** — king of Babylon; the instrument of God's judgment against Judah.

---

### Jerusalem

The destruction of Jerusalem and the Temple is the defining catastrophe of the entire Old Testament — and it happens in this book. Everything in the narrative of [[Genesis]] through [[1 Kings]] builds toward this moment of judgment; everything in [[Ezra]], [[Nehemiah]], the prophets, and the Psalms responds to it. The Temple — the place where heaven and earth met, where the glory of God dwelt — is reduced to ash. The Davidic king is blinded and deported. The city that was to be the center of God's purposes on earth lies in ruins. The theological weight of 2 Kings 25 cannot be overstated.

---

### Connection to Jesus

Jesus explicitly places himself in the Elijah/Elisha tradition. In Luke 4:24–27, inaugurating his ministry in Nazareth, he cites Naaman's healing and the widow of Zarephath as precedents — grace extended to outsiders when Israel was passed over. This was so offensive to his hometown audience they tried to throw him off a cliff. The Babylonian exile is embedded in Jesus's genealogy in Matthew 1 as a watershed moment dividing Israel's history, making clear that the story of covenant failure and promised restoration is the story into which Jesus steps as the one who finally keeps what Israel could not.
