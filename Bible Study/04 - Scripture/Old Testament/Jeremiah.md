# Jeremiah

**Overview:** Jeremiah is called before birth to deliver a message of judgment to Jerusalem during its final decades before the Babylonian conquest. He preaches for forty years, is ignored, mocked, and imprisoned, watches everything he warned about come true, and stays in the ruins when everyone else is taken away. Among the prophets he is the most autobiographically exposed — his private anguish is part of the text — and he announces the new covenant, the most theologically significant promise in the Old Testament.

---

### Key Moments

- **The Call — *"Before I formed you in the womb I knew you"* (Jer 1:5):** God commissions Jeremiah before birth, appointing him *"a prophet to the nations."* Jeremiah protests his youth; God touches his mouth and sends him anyway. The call establishes the book's central tension: a man who does not want this vocation and cannot escape it.

- **The Temple Sermon (Jer 7):** Jeremiah stands at the gate of the Temple and dismantles the popular assumption that God's presence guarantees Jerusalem's protection. *"Do not trust in deceptive words: 'This is the temple of the LORD, the temple of the LORD, the temple of the LORD.'"* The Temple has become a lucky charm; God will destroy it as he destroyed Shiloh.

- **The Potter and the Clay (Jer 18):** Jeremiah watches a potter rework a spoiled vessel and hears God's sovereignty declared in it — God can reshape or unmake a nation at any time. The passage has no soft edges: it is a warning to those who assume continuity with the divine plan.

- **The Confessions (Jer 11; 15; 17; 18; 20):** A series of brutally candid prayers in which Jeremiah accuses God of deceiving him, complains that the wicked prosper, and wishes he had never been born — *"Cursed be the day on which I was born"* (Jer 20:14). Nowhere else in the prophets is the internal cost of the prophetic vocation so directly recorded. These are not polished liturgy; they are wrestling.

- **Jeremiah Buys a Field (Jer 32):** With Babylon's army literally surrounding Jerusalem, Jeremiah purchases a field in Anathoth from his cousin. He signs the deed, seals it, and stores it — a deliberate act of prophetic hope. *"Houses and fields and vineyards shall again be bought in this land."* It is one of the most arresting gestures in the OT: an investment in a future the buyer will not live to see.

- **The New Covenant (Jer 31:31–34):** The theological summit of the book. *"I will put my law within them and write it on their hearts; and I will be their God, and they shall be my people."* The old covenant written in stone and broken repeatedly will be replaced by an inward covenant of transformed hearts. God will forgive and forget: *"I will remember their sin no more."*

- **Jerusalem Falls (Jer 39–40):** The Babylonians breach the walls, burn the Temple, and take the people into exile. Jeremiah is released by Nebuchadnezzar's orders and given a choice: go to Babylon with the deportees, or stay. He stays in the ruins with Gedaliah and the remnant left in the land.

- **Taken to Egypt Against His Will (Jer 43–44):** The remnant, against Jeremiah's explicit prophetic warning, flees to Egypt after Gedaliah is assassinated. They take Jeremiah with them. He prophesies there against them and against Egypt. His final fate is not recorded — the book closes without resolution. He disappears into Egypt, still speaking, still unheeded.

### Main Characters

**Jeremiah** — The reluctant prophet, called before birth, whose forty-year ministry spans the reigns of Josiah through the fall of Jerusalem. His internal life is more visible than any other prophet's; the book interweaves his preaching with his private anguish. **Baruch** — Jeremiah's secretary and closest companion, who writes and reads the scrolls when Jeremiah is barred from the Temple (Jer 36). He preserves the text; a short oracle is addressed to him personally (Jer 45). **Josiah** — The reforming king under whom Jeremiah begins his ministry; his death at Megiddo (609 BC) removes the last political buffer against disaster. **Jehoiakim** — Josiah's son, who burns Jeremiah's scroll strip by strip as it is read to him (Jer 36) — the most brazen act of defiance against a prophetic word in the OT. **Zedekiah** — The last king of Judah; weak, vacillating, secretly sympathetic to Jeremiah, but ultimately unwilling to act on what he hears. He watches his sons killed before being blinded and taken to Babylon. **Nebuchadnezzar** — The Babylonian king whom Jeremiah calls, provocatively, *"my servant"* (Jer 25:9) — God's instrument of judgment. **Ebed-melech** — An Ethiopian royal official who pulls Jeremiah out of the cistern where he has been left to die (Jer 38). He is personally promised protection when Jerusalem falls (Jer 39:15–18).

---

### Jerusalem

Jeremiah is inseparable from Jerusalem. He preaches in its gates and Temple courts, is imprisoned in its palace complex, watches it besieged and burned, and stays in its ruins when everyone else is carried away. No prophet feels Jerusalem's destruction more personally. His early oracle *"Hast thou utterly rejected Judah? Does thy soul loathe Zion?"* (Jer 14:19) and the grief threaded through the confessions give the city an emotional weight in this book that anticipates [[Lamentations]]. The promise of restoration in Jeremiah 30–33 — the *"Book of Consolation"* — is geographically specific: exiles will return to Jerusalem, the city will be rebuilt, the land repurchased. The field at Anathoth is both sign and pledge.

---

### Connection to Jesus

Jeremiah 31:31–34 is quoted in full in Hebrews 8:8–12 — the longest OT quotation in the New Testament — and cited again in Hebrews 10:16–17. The author's argument is explicit: the new covenant Jesus enacts supersedes the Mosaic covenant, fulfilling exactly what Jeremiah announced. At the Last Supper, Jesus takes the cup and says *"this cup is the new covenant in my blood"* (Luke 22:20; 1 Cor 11:25) — he is quoting Jeremiah 31:31 and applying it to himself. Matthew sees Jesus's flight to Egypt and return as fulfilling Jeremiah 31:15 (*"Rachel weeping for her children"*) — a passage about the exile, reread as a type of the slaughter of the innocents. Jesus's lament over Jerusalem — *"O Jerusalem, Jerusalem, the city that kills the prophets"* (Matt 23:37) — echoes the pattern of Jeremiah's entire ministry: a prophet who loved the city, was rejected by it, and stayed with it in its grief. Early Christians identified Jesus as *"Jeremiah or one of the prophets"* (Matt 16:14).
