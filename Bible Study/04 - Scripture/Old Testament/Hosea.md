# Hosea

**Overview:** God commands Hosea to marry a woman named Gomer who will be unfaithful to him, then commands him to take her back when she leaves. This is not metaphor first and experience second — Hosea lives it, and through his marriage God dramatises the covenant relationship with Israel: the pursuing love, the betrayal, the anguish, the refusal to give up. It is one of the emotionally rawest books in the Old Testament, and its vision of divine love as wounded, persisting, and ultimately irresistible reshapes how the prophetic tradition speaks of God.

---

### Key Moments

- **The Command to Marry Gomer (Hos 1):** God's opening command: *"Go, take to yourself a wife of whoredom and have children of whoredom, for the land commits great whoredom by forsaking the LORD."* Hosea marries Gomer. She bears three children, each given a name that is a prophetic verdict: Jezreel (judgment on the dynasty), Lo-Ruhamah (*"No Mercy"* — God withdrawing compassion from Israel), Lo-Ammi (*"Not My People"* — the covenant publicly disowned). The names are escalating; the covenant dissolution is being named into existence.

- **The Command to Take Gomer Back (Hos 3):** Gomer has left Hosea and ended up in what appears to be sexual slavery or destitution. God's command: *"Go again, love a woman who is loved by another man and is an adulteress, even as the LORD loves the children of Israel, though they turn to other gods."* Hosea buys her back for fifteen shekels of silver and some barley. The purchase price, not her beauty or virtue, is what drives the narrative. It is an act of sheer costly will.

- **Israel's Apostasy as Adultery (Hos 2; 4; 8):** Hosea sustains the marriage metaphor through detailed indictments. Israel has gone after the Baals, credited the fertility gods with the grain and wine and oil that the LORD provided (Hos 2:8). The priests profit from Israel's sin rather than confronting it (Hos 4:8). The people make idols with their silver, calling them gods (Hos 8:4–6). The metaphor is not softened into symbolism — it is pressed hard.

- **God's Anguish (Hos 11:8):** *"How can I give you up, O Ephraim? How can I hand you over, O Israel? How can I make you like Admah? How can I treat you like Zeboiim? My heart recoils within me; my compassion grows warm and tender."* This is one of the most theologically bold passages in the entire OT — God describing himself as caught between justice and love, his compassion overriding his anger. *"I will not execute my burning anger; I will not again destroy Ephraim; for I am God and not a man."*

- **Desire Over Sacrifice (Hos 6:6):** *"For I desire steadfast love and not sacrifice, the knowledge of God rather than burnt offerings."* Israel's cultic life has become a substitute for faithfulness. The prophetic critique here is not against the sacrificial system per se but against the assumption that ritual can replace the relationship it was designed to express. This verse becomes a touchstone in the Gospels.

- **The Promise of Healing (Hos 14:4):** The book closes with one of the most tender divine promises in the prophets: *"I will heal their apostasy; I will love them freely, for my anger has turned from them."* After forty chapters of indictment and grief, God does not end with judgment. He ends with the offer that was always underneath the whole book.

### Main Characters

**Hosea** — Prophet of the northern kingdom (Israel/Ephraim) in the eighth century BC, contemporary with [[Amos]] in the north and [[Isaiah]] in the south. His marriage is the vehicle of his entire message; his personal anguish and the divine anguish are inseparable in the text. **Gomer** — Hosea's wife; she is not given a voice in the text, which has generated substantial interpretive debate about the ethics of the narrative. She functions typologically as Israel — not merely as a symbol but as a real person through whom the type is enacted. **The three children** — Jezreel, Lo-Ruhamah, Lo-Ammi; their names are their message, and in Hos 1:10–2:1 their names are reversed as a promise of restoration: *"You are my people"* and *"You have received mercy."*

---

### Connection to Jesus

*"Out of Egypt I called my son"* (Hos 11:1) is explicitly quoted in Matthew 2:15 to describe the return of the infant Jesus from Egypt. Hosea's original referent is Israel's exodus; Matthew reads it typologically — Israel's experience is recapitulated in the Messiah's life. The type is not abandoned but fulfilled in a person. Hosea 6:6 — *"I desire steadfast love and not sacrifice"* — is quoted by Jesus twice in Matthew's Gospel (Matt 9:13 and Matt 12:7), both times in conflict situations where religious practitioners are privileging observance over mercy. Jesus is directly applying Hosea's criterion. The pattern of the book — faithful love pursuing and redeeming an unfaithful spouse at personal cost — is taken up in Ephesians 5:25–32 as the governing metaphor for Christ's love for the church.
