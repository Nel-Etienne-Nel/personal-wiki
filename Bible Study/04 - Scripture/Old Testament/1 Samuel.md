# 1 Samuel

**Overview:** The hinge book between the period of the judges and the monarchy. Samuel serves as the last great judge and the first major prophet — the transitional figure who anoints two kings. Israel demands a king to be like the nations; God gives them Saul, who starts with promise and ends in disobedience and spiritual ruin. In his place, God chooses David — a shepherd, the youngest of eight brothers — and anoints him while Saul still sits on the throne. The tension between the rejected king and the chosen king runs through the book's second half. [[2 Samuel]] picks up where this ends.

---

### Key Moments

- **Hannah's Prayer and Samuel's Birth (1 Sam 1–2):** Hannah is barren and deeply distressed; she vows her child to God if he grants her a son. Samuel is born, weaned, and brought to Eli at the tabernacle in Shiloh. Hannah's prayer of praise anticipates Mary's Magnificat in structure and theology: God brings down the mighty and raises the lowly, he guards the feet of his faithful ones.

- **The Ark Captured and Returned (1 Sam 4–6):** Israel treats the Ark as a battle talisman; the Philistines capture it and place it in Dagon's temple. Dagon's statue falls before the Ark twice, ending up headless and handless. Disaster follows the Ark through every Philistine city. After seven months, the Philistines send it back on a cart driven by two untrained cows who walk straight toward Israel without prompting.

- **Israel Demands a King (1 Sam 8):** The elders ask Samuel for a king "like all the nations." God tells Samuel this is a rejection of God's own kingship, not Samuel's leadership. God grants the request but warns explicitly of what a king will take from the people — their sons, daughters, fields, and a tenth of everything. *"And in that day you will cry out because of your king, whom you have chosen for yourselves, but the LORD will not answer you in that day"* (8:18).

- **Saul Anointed (1 Sam 9–10):** Saul, a head taller than anyone in Israel and from the tribe of Benjamin, is privately anointed by Samuel and then publicly chosen by lot before all Israel. He hides among the baggage when his name is drawn. The beginning is auspicious.

- **Saul's Disobedience (1 Sam 15):** Saul is commanded to destroy the Amalekites and everything they have. He spares King Agag and the best livestock. His excuse: he saved them for sacrifice. Samuel's response is decisive — *"Has the LORD as great delight in burnt offerings and sacrifices, as in obeying the voice of the LORD? Behold, to obey is better than sacrifice"* (15:22). God rejects Saul as king. Samuel hacks Agag to pieces before the LORD.

- **David Anointed (1 Sam 16):** God sends Samuel to Jesse's house in Bethlehem to anoint one of his sons. Jesse parades seven sons before Samuel; God rejects each one. The eighth — the youngest, out in the fields with the sheep — is David. *"The LORD sees not as man sees: man looks on the outward appearance, but the LORD looks on the heart"* (16:7).

- **David and Goliath (1 Sam 17):** Goliath, a Philistine giant, issues a daily challenge for forty days. No Israelite will face him. David, delivering food to his brothers, hears the challenge and volunteers. He refuses Saul's armor; he takes five smooth stones and a sling. He kills Goliath with the first stone. The victory is won before the armies engage — the battle belongs to God.

- **David and Jonathan (1 Sam 18–20):** Jonathan, Saul's son and the crown prince, enters a covenant of friendship with David — giving him his robe, armor, and weapons. As Saul's jealousy grows violent, Jonathan repeatedly shields David at personal risk, despite knowing David will inherit the throne he should occupy.

- **David Spares Saul (1 Sam 24, 26):** Twice David has Saul within his power — once in a cave, once at night in his camp. Both times David refuses to harm *"the LORD's anointed,"* cutting a corner of his robe instead in one instance. His restraint is theological: God put Saul in place; only God removes him.

- **The Witch of Endor (1 Sam 28):** With Samuel dead and God silent, Saul secretly consults a medium at Endor. The woman brings up Samuel — apparently actually Samuel — who confirms that the battle tomorrow will be Saul's end.

- **Saul's Death on Mount Gilboa (1 Sam 31):** Saul's sons, including Jonathan, are killed in battle. Saul is wounded by archers and falls on his own sword rather than be captured. His body is hung on the wall of Beth-shan. The men of Jabesh-gilead, whom Saul had once delivered, march through the night and retrieve the bodies for burial.

### Main Characters

**Samuel** — Prophet, judge, priest-like figure; the last man who embodies all three offices before they begin to separate. His word never falls to the ground (3:19). **Eli** — The aging, corrupt priest at Shiloh whose sons dishonor God's offerings; God's judgment on his house frames Samuel's rise. **Saul** — Tall, impressive, genuinely gifted and initially humble, undone by a pattern of partial obedience and fear of the people's opinion over God's command. **Jonathan** — One of the most morally clear figures in the OT; his friendship with David is an act of covenant faithfulness despite it costing him his inheritance. **David** — Shepherd, musician, soldier, poet; chosen for his heart rather than his appearance. The book follows his rise from anointing to the brink of kingship. **Goliath** — The Philistine champion whose defiance of God's army is the foil for David's God-centered confidence. **Abigail** — The intelligent wife of Nabal who intercepts David's revenge mission, speaks wisely, and later becomes David's wife after Nabal's death.

---

### Connection to Jesus

David is the OT figure most directly and most frequently connected to Jesus. *"Son of David"* becomes the primary messianic title in the NT — used by blind men at roadsides (Matt 9:27), by the crowd at the triumphal entry (Matt 21:9), and debated by Jesus himself in the temple (Matt 22:41–46). David's anointing as true king while Saul still occupies the throne, the years of waiting and suffering before his coronation, his raising up of the outcast and the despised — all of it creates the pattern that Jesus fulfills. Hannah's prayer at the book's opening, with its language of reversal and the LORD's anointed (1 Sam 2:10), is the direct literary template for Mary's Magnificat (Luke 1:46–55).
