# 1 Kings

**Overview:** The arc runs from glory to fracture. Solomon's reign opens in brilliance — the Temple is built, wisdom is legendary, nations come to hear him — but his foreign marriages draw him into idolatry, and God tears the kingdom from his son's hands. After the split, the northern kingdom of Israel descends rapidly into apostasy under Jeroboam's state-sponsored idol worship. The book ends in the north with the reign of Ahab, whose marriage to the Phoenician princess Jezebel makes the confrontation with the prophet Elijah inevitable. [[2 Kings]] continues directly.

---

### Key Moments

- **Solomon Asks for Wisdom (1 Kings 3):** God appears to Solomon at Gibeon and offers him whatever he asks. Solomon asks for a discerning heart to govern the people and to tell good from evil. God gives wisdom and adds riches and honor besides. Solomon's judgment over the two women and the disputed baby (3:16–28) immediately demonstrates it — *"all Israel heard of the judgment... and they stood in awe of the king, because they perceived that the wisdom of God was in him"* (3:28).

- **The Temple Built (1 Kings 6–7):** Construction begins in Solomon's fourth year and takes seven years. The account is detailed to an unusual degree — dimensions, materials, carvings, the cherubim in the inner sanctuary, the bronze sea, the ten stands, the two pillars named Jachin and Boaz. The Temple is the fulfillment of what David desired ([[2 Samuel]] 7) and what all of [[Deuteronomy]] 12's "the place the LORD will choose" pointed toward.

- **The Glory Fills the Temple (1 Kings 8):** When the priests bring the Ark into the Most Holy Place and withdraw, a cloud fills the Temple so thick the priests cannot stand to minister. *"The glory of the LORD filled the house of the LORD"* (8:11). Solomon's dedicatory prayer is one of the great prayers of the OT — he prays for every future category of need: individual sin, national defeat, drought, foreign supplicant, exile. His theology of prayer is specific and capacious.

- **Solomon's Foreign Wives and Idolatry (1 Kings 11):** Solomon loved many foreign women — seven hundred wives of royal birth and three hundred concubines. They turned his heart after their gods. He built high places for Chemosh and Molech and the gods of all his wives. *"So Solomon did what was evil in the sight of the LORD"* (11:6). God announces the kingdom will be torn from his son's hand — not all of it, for David's sake, but most of it.

- **The Kingdom Splits (1 Kings 12):** Rehoboam, Solomon's son, is approached by all Israel asking for relief from the heavy labor his father imposed. The elders advise conciliation; Rehoboam's young friends advise doubling down. He listens to the young men. Ten tribes withdraw under Jeroboam; only Judah and Benjamin remain with the house of David. The united kingdom never reunites.

- **Jeroboam's Golden Calves (1 Kings 12):** Jeroboam, king of the north, fears that if his people travel to Jerusalem to worship, their loyalty will shift back to Rehoboam. He sets up two golden calves — one at Bethel, one at Dan — and says, *"Here are your gods, O Israel, who brought you up out of Egypt"* (12:28). The echo of Aaron's calf in [[Exodus]] 32 is deliberate. This becomes the founding sin of the northern kingdom; every northern king is measured against it.

- **Elijah and the Drought (1 Kings 17):** Elijah the Tishbite appears without introduction and announces to Ahab that there will be neither dew nor rain except by his word. He is hidden by God at the Wadi Cherith (fed by ravens) and then with a widow in Zarephath, where he raises her dead son.

- **The Contest on Mount Carmel (1 Kings 18):** Elijah confronts 450 prophets of Baal and 400 prophets of Asherah. Two bulls are prepared on altars; each side calls on its god to send fire. Baal's prophets cry out all morning and cut themselves; nothing happens. Elijah taunts them. He rebuilds the LORD's altar with twelve stones, digs a trench, and drenches the wood with water three times. He prays once, briefly. Fire falls and consumes the offering, the wood, the stones, the dust, and the water in the trench. The prophets of Baal are seized and killed. Rain comes.

- **Elijah Under the Juniper Tree (1 Kings 19):** Immediately after Carmel, Jezebel threatens Elijah's life and he runs — a day's journey into the wilderness, sits under a broom tree, and asks to die. *"It is enough; now, O LORD, take away my life"* (19:4). An angel wakes him, feeds him twice, and sends him forty days to Horeb. There God speaks — not in the great wind, earthquake, or fire, but in a low whisper. God restores Elijah and gives him a commission: anoint Hazael, Jehu, and Elisha.

- **Naboth's Vineyard (1 Kings 21):** Ahab wants Naboth's vineyard adjacent to his palace. Naboth refuses to sell his ancestral inheritance. Ahab sulks; Jezebel arranges false witnesses who accuse Naboth of blasphemy; Naboth is stoned. Ahab takes the vineyard. Elijah meets him there: *"Have you killed and also taken possession?"* God's judgment is pronounced — dogs will lick Ahab's blood where they licked Naboth's, and Jezebel will be eaten by dogs at the wall of Jezreel.

### Main Characters

**Solomon** — The wisest man who ever lived, whose wisdom did not prevent him from the oldest foolishness. His reign is the apex of Israel's earthly glory and the pivot point of its decline. **Rehoboam** — Solomon's son; his foolishness is the proximate cause of the split, though God is clear this is his doing to fulfill his word (12:15). **Jeroboam** — The first king of northern Israel; his golden calves define the northern kingdom's apostasy for its entire 200-year existence. **Ahab** — The worst king of the north to that point, not least because of who he married. He is not a cartoon villain — he sulks over the vineyard, humbles himself when confronted — but he consistently capitulates to Jezebel's agenda. **Jezebel** — The daughter of the king of Sidon; ruthless, politically capable, and a dedicated promoter of Baal worship. She is the dominant force behind Ahab's reign. **Elijah** — The most dramatic prophet in the OT; his ministry is fire, drought, and directness. He confronts kings, raises the dead, and experiences profound despair. He is the type of the returning prophet promised in Malachi. **Obadiah** — Ahab's palace administrator who secretly hides one hundred prophets of the LORD in two caves during Jezebel's purge; faith operating quietly inside a hostile court.

---

### Jerusalem

The Temple is built and dedicated in Jerusalem in 1 Kings 6–8 — the singular theological event of the book and one of the most significant in the entire OT. For the next four centuries, the Temple is the dwelling place of God's name among his people, the destination of three annual pilgrimage feasts, the place where the glory cloud rests. [[Deuteronomy]] 12's unnamed "place the LORD will choose" is here given its permanent address. The Temple built in this book is where Jesus will teach, cleanse, and weep over the city — and where the veil will tear from top to bottom at his death.

---

### Connection to Jesus

Solomon's wisdom is directly invoked by Jesus: *"the queen of the South came from the ends of the earth to hear the wisdom of Solomon, and behold, something greater than Solomon is here"* (Matt 12:42). The Temple — built here, the central object of 1 Kings — is the location of some of Jesus's most significant actions: his parents find him there at twelve, he teaches there throughout his ministry, he drives out the merchants, he weeps over Jerusalem from the Mount of Olives. When he dies, the Temple veil — the boundary between the Holy Place and the Most Holy Place, the architectural expression of the distance between God and sinful man — tears in two.

Elijah's expected return, promised in Malachi 4:5 (*"Behold, I will send you Elijah the prophet before the great and awesome day of the LORD comes"*), is fulfilled in John the Baptist. Jesus says it directly: *"if you are willing to accept it, he is Elijah who is to come"* (Matt 11:14). At the Transfiguration, Elijah appears with Moses alongside Jesus on the mountain — the Law and the Prophets in person, attesting to the one they pointed toward.
