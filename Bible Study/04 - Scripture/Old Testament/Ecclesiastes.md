# Ecclesiastes

**Overview:** Qohelet — the Preacher or Teacher — has conducted a systematic investigation of everything human life can offer: wisdom, pleasure, great building projects, wealth, work, fame. His verdict is *hebel*: vapor, breath, mist — things that evaporate in your hands. Not nihilism, but relentless realism about what life "under the sun" can and cannot deliver. The book's final move is not despair but redirection.

---

### Key Moments

- **The Opening Declaration (Eccl 1:2):** *"Vanity of vanities, says the Preacher, vanity of vanities! All is vanity."* The Hebrew *hebel* (breath, vapor) appears 38 times in the book. This is not cynicism — it is precision. Things that are breath-like are not evil; they are simply unable to bear the weight we put on them.

- **The Experiments (Eccl 1–2):** Qohelet tests wisdom, laughter, wine, great building works, gardens, slaves, singers, wealth — *"whatever my eyes desired I did not keep from them."* Each experiment ends the same way: *hebel*. Not because these things are worthless, but because they cannot answer the deep question.

- **A Time for Everything (Eccl 3:1–8):** *"For everything there is a season, and a time for every matter under heaven: a time to be born and a time to die..."* One of the most quoted passages in the Bible — but in context, it is not comfort. It is evidence that humans cannot control the timing of their own lives. God holds time; we do not.

- **The Superiority of Mourning (Eccl 7:2):** *"It is better to go to the house of mourning than to go to the house of feasting, for this is the end of all mankind, and the living will lay it to heart."* Honesty about death is more formative than distraction from it.

- **Enjoy Life While You Have It (Eccl 9:7–10):** *"Go, eat your bread with joy, and drink your wine with a merry heart, for God has already approved what you do."* Recurring in the book — not hedonism but a proper enjoyment of genuine goods as gifts, without demanding they be ultimate.

- **Remember Your Creator in Youth (Eccl 12:1):** *"Remember also your Creator in the days of your youth, before the evil days come and the years draw near of which you will say, 'I have no pleasure in them.'"* The extended metaphor of aging that follows (12:2–7) is one of the most powerful poems in the OT.

- **The Conclusion (Eccl 12:13–14):** *"Fear God and keep his commandments, for this is the whole duty of man. For God will bring every deed into judgment, with every secret thing, whether good or evil."* The investigation ends not with despair but with a return to the beginning — the fear of the LORD (cf. [[Proverbs]] 9:10). Everything else has been cleared away to make room for this.

---

### Main Characters

**Qohelet** — The Teacher/Preacher; the narrator and investigator. Traditionally identified with Solomon, given the references to unparalleled wisdom and wealth (Eccl 1:16, 2:4–9), though the book never names him directly. The literary persona of a man who had every advantage and still found the question unanswered — which is precisely what gives his conclusions weight.

---

### Connection to Jesus

Ecclesiastes diagnoses the condition that the Gospel addresses. The book's verdict — that life lived in pursuit of anything "under the sun" ends in *hebel* — is the anthropological problem. Jesus's words *"seek first the kingdom of God"* (Matt 6:33) and *"what does it profit a man to gain the whole world and forfeit his soul?"* (Mark 8:36) operate on the same ground. Paul's account of creation *"subjected to futility"* and groaning for redemption (Rom 8:20) echoes Ecclesiastes directly. The book clears the field; the NT fills it.
