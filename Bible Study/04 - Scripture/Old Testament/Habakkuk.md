# Habakkuk

**Overview:** A prophet who does not deliver a message to the people but argues directly with God. He brings two complaints — why is wickedness in Judah unpunished, and why will God use a more wicked nation to punish a less wicked one? — and receives two answers. The book closes with one of the most remarkable declarations of naked faith in all of Scripture, and its central verse becomes the foundation stone of Pauline theology.

---

### Key Moments

- **The first complaint (Hab 1:2–4):** *"O LORD, how long shall I cry for help, and you will not hear? Or cry to you 'Violence!' and you will not save?"* The law is paralysed, justice is perverted, the wicked surround the righteous. Habakkuk does not ask about personal suffering — he asks about systemic injustice and God's apparent indifference.

- **God's shocking answer (Hab 1:5–11):** *"Look among the nations, and see; wonder and be astounded. For I am doing a work in your days that you would not believe if told."* God will raise up the Babylonians — *"that bitter and hasty nation"* — to sweep through the land. The answer is worse than the silence.

- **The second complaint (Hab 1:12–2:1):** Habakkuk is theologically precise in his protest. God is too pure to look at evil — so how can he use a more wicked instrument to punish a less wicked one? He takes his stand on the watchtower and waits. *"I will take my post; I will look out to see what he will say to me."*

- **The LORD's answer — and the verse that changes everything (Hab 2:2–4):** God tells Habakkuk to write the vision plainly — it is for an appointed time, not yet. Then: *"Behold, his soul is puffed up; it is not upright within him, but the righteous shall live by his faith."* One clause, eight words in English, that Paul will build a theology upon.

- **The five woes against Babylon (Hab 2:6–20):** Woe to the one who heaps up plunder, who builds a city with bloodshed, who makes neighbours drunk to expose them, who worships idols of wood. The logic is inescapable: the standard Habakkuk applied to Judah applies equally to Babylon. Justice is not special pleading — it will reach every oppressor.

- **Habakkuk's psalm (Hab 3):** A hymn recalling God's appearance at Sinai, his ancient victories — the earth trembles, nations scatter, rivers dry up. Then the arrival: *"I hear, and my body trembles; my lips quiver at the sound... Yet I will quietly wait for the day of trouble to come upon people who invade us."* And the conclusion — the most astonishing statement of faith in conditions of total deprivation: *"Though the fig tree should not blossom, nor fruit be on the vines, the produce of the olive fail and the fields yield no food, the flock be cut off from the fold and there be no herd in the stalls, yet I will rejoice in the LORD; I will take joy in the God of my salvation"* (Hab 3:17–18).

---

### Main Characters

**Habakkuk** — One of the most theologically sophisticated voices among the prophets. He does not merely announce what God says; he argues, waits, protests, and ultimately trusts — not because his questions are answered but because he has encountered God directly.

---

### Connection to Jesus

*"The righteous shall live by his faith"* (Hab 2:4) is Paul's foundational OT text for justification by faith. He quotes it in Romans 1:17 as the thesis of the entire letter, in Galatians 3:11 as the direct refutation of justification by law, and the writer of Hebrews uses it in 10:38 in the context of perseverance under trial. One half-verse in an obscure prophetic dialogue becomes the hinge on which Reformation theology turns. Habakkuk 1:5 is also quoted by Paul in Acts 13:41 as a warning to those who reject the gospel — God is doing a work that would not be believed even if told, and the Resurrection is that work.
