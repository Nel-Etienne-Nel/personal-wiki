# Ezekiel

**Overview:** Ezekiel is a priest taken into Babylonian exile with the first deportation in 597 BC, before Jerusalem falls. Beside the Chebar canal in Babylon, he receives the most visually overwhelming prophetic visions in the Old Testament. His central burden is the departure of God's glory from the Temple — the most solemn theological event in the OT — followed by the long prophetic arc toward its return. The book moves from judgment to restoration, from exile to a detailed vision of a reconstituted Temple and renewed land.

---

### Key Moments

- **The Merkavah Vision — The Chariot of God (Ezek 1):** Ezekiel's inaugural vision: a storm cloud from the north, four living creatures (each with four faces: lion, ox, man, eagle), wheels within wheels whose rims are full of eyes, and above them a firmament and a throne of sapphire — and on the throne *"a likeness with a human appearance"* surrounded by fire and rainbow radiance. This is the closest the OT comes to a direct vision of God. It became the foundation of Jewish mystical tradition (Merkavah mysticism).

- **Eating the Scroll (Ezek 3):** God hands Ezekiel a scroll covered in words of lamentation and gives him a command: eat it. Ezekiel eats it, and it is *"sweet as honey in my mouth."* God's word must be internalized before it is proclaimed. But the message Ezekiel is sent with will not be received: Israel is *"a rebellious house."*

- **Acted Parables of Judgment (Ezek 4–5):** Ezekiel is commanded to act out the siege of Jerusalem in miniature — lying on his side for 390 days for Israel's sins, then 40 days for Judah's; eating food cooked over dung; shaving his head and dividing the hair to show what will happen to Jerusalem's population. The prophet's body becomes the prophetic medium.

- **The Glory Departs from the Temple (Ezek 10–11):** Ezekiel watches the glory of the LORD — the *kavod* — abandon the Temple in stages. It moves from the Holy of Holies to the threshold, to the east gate, then to the Mount of Olives, and finally departs eastward toward Babylon. This is the theological catastrophe that makes the physical destruction of 586 BC intelligible. The building falls because God has already left it. It is one of the most solemn scenes in all of Scripture.

- **Jerusalem as an Unfaithful Wife (Ezek 16):** An extended and unflinching allegory in which Jerusalem is a foundling child adopted and betrothed by God, who becomes more corrupt than Sodom or Samaria. The allegory does not soften the indictment. It ends, against all expectation, with an unconditional promise of restoration: *"I will establish my covenant with you, and you shall know that I am the LORD."*

- **The Valley of Dry Bones (Ezek 37):** God sets Ezekiel in a valley of dry bones — *"very many, and very dry"* — and asks: *"Can these bones live?"* Ezekiel prophesies over them; sinew, flesh, and skin cover them; God breathes into them. An army stands. The vision is explicitly interpreted as national resurrection: the whole house of Israel brought back from exile and given new life. *"I will put my Spirit within you, and you shall live."*

- **Gog and Magog (Ezek 38–39):** A prophecy of a great northern coalition that will invade a restored Israel in the *"latter days"* and be destroyed by God himself. The identity of Gog is disputed throughout interpretive history; the passage established apocalyptic categories that run through Revelation 20.

- **The Vision of the New Temple (Ezek 40–48):** A meticulous architectural vision of a future Temple, with precise measurements of every gate, court, chamber, and wall. The climax: the glory of the LORD returns from the east through the eastern gate — reversing its departure in Ezek 10–11 — and fills the Temple. *"This is the place of my throne and the place of the soles of my feet, where I will dwell in the midst of the people of Israel forever."* A river flows from the Temple, growing into an uncrossable river that brings life to the Dead Sea.

---

### Main Characters

**Ezekiel** — Priest and prophet; the most physically involved of all the prophets, performing extended enacted parables in his own body. He is addressed by God as *"Son of Man"* (ben adam) ninety-three times — a reminder of his creatureliness before the overwhelming divine presence. **Ezekiel's wife** — She dies suddenly; God forbids Ezekiel to mourn her as a sign that Israel will lose the Temple (its delight) and be too stunned to mourn (Ezek 24). Her death is a prophetic act. **The four living creatures** — The cherubim who bear the divine throne; they appear in both the inaugural vision (Ezek 1) and the Temple departure (Ezek 10).

---

### Jerusalem

Ezekiel watches Jerusalem fall from exile in Babylon, receiving news of it chapter by chapter. His entire prophetic horizon is the city's fate: the judgment that makes it fall, the departure of the divine presence that preceded it, and the detailed vision of its future reconstitution. The departure and return of God's glory — both keyed to Jerusalem and its Temple — are the theological pillars of the book. His Temple vision in Ezek 40–48 is the most detailed description of Jerusalem's future in the entire OT.

---

### Connection to Jesus

The four living creatures of Ezekiel 1 — lion, ox, man, eagle — become in early Christian tradition the symbols of the four Gospels (Matthew, Luke, Mark, John respectively in Irenaeus's assignment, later rearranged). *"Son of Man"* (*ben adam*), used ninety-three times in Ezekiel as God's form of address to the prophet, becomes Jesus's preferred self-designation — loading it with both creatureliness and, via [[Daniel]] 7:13, cosmic authority. The life-giving river flowing from the Temple in Ezek 47 is echoed in Jesus's promise of *"rivers of living water"* (John 7:38) and in the river of life from the throne in Revelation 22. The returning glory (Ezek 43) finds resonance at the Transfiguration, where the divine kavod settles on Jesus. Ezek 36:26–27 — *"I will give you a new heart... and put my Spirit within you"* — is the pneumatological complement to [[Jeremiah]]'s new covenant promise, both fulfilled at Pentecost.
