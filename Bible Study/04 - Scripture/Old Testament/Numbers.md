# Numbers

**Overview:** Israel's forty years in the wilderness between Sinai and Canaan. The book covers two censuses (hence the name), a series of rebellions, and the slow dying-out of the exodus generation who refused to trust God at the threshold of the Promised Land. A generation forfeits its inheritance through unbelief.

---

### Key Moments

- **The two censuses (Num 1, 26):** One taken at Sinai at the start; one on the plains of Moab at the end — the bookends of a lost generation
- **The twelve spies (Num 13–14):** Sent into Canaan; ten return with fear ("we were like grasshoppers"); two (Caleb and Joshua) with faith. The people side with the fearful
- **God's judgment (Num 14):** The entire exodus generation condemned to wander until they die; their children will enter the land they forfeited
- **Korah's rebellion (Num 16):** A Levite leads a coalition against Moses; the ground opens and swallows him and his household; 250 leaders consumed by fire
- **Moses strikes the rock (Num 20):** God tells him to speak to the rock; Moses strikes it in anger instead. Water comes — but God bars Moses from entering the Promised Land for this act
- **The bronze serpent (Num 21):** God sends venomous snakes as judgment; Moses makes a bronze serpent on a pole; anyone bitten who looks at it lives
- **Balaam and his donkey (Num 22–24):** A pagan diviner hired to curse Israel speaks only blessing; his donkey sees the angel of the LORD before he does; his oracles include a star-prophecy over Israel

---

### Main Characters

**Moses** — leading, interceding, and in this book, finally failing. **Aaron** — dies on Mount Hor (Num 20). **Miriam** — dies in the wilderness (Num 20). **Caleb and Joshua** — the two faithful spies; the only adults of the exodus generation who will enter Canaan. **Korah** — the rebel Levite. **Balaam** — the morally compromised prophet who cannot curse what God has blessed. **Phinehas** — Aaron's grandson; his zeal for holiness during Israel's idolatry at Peor earns a covenant of priesthood.

---

### Connection to Jesus

Jesus directly references the bronze serpent in John 3:14 — *"as Moses lifted up the serpent in the wilderness, so must the Son of Man be lifted up"* — mapping it explicitly onto the crucifixion. Paul uses the wilderness rebellion stories as warnings to the Corinthian church (1 Cor 10:1–11), calling the rock that followed them *"Christ."* The generation that could not enter rest due to unbelief is the central illustration in Hebrews 3–4.
