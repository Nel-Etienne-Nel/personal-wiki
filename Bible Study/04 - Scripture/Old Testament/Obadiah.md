# Obadiah

**Overview:** The shortest book in the Old Testament — one chapter, 21 verses. A single sustained prophecy against Edom, the nation descended from Esau, for gloating over and actively profiting from Jerusalem's destruction. Obadiah situates the particular cruelty of Edom's betrayal — not a foreign enemy but a brother nation — within the larger framework of the Day of the LORD, when all nations will be held to account.

---

### Key Moments

- **Edom's pride indicted (Obad 1–9):** Edom dwells in the clefts of the rock, high and secure, saying *"Who will bring me down to the ground?"* The oracle begins by demolishing that confidence — allies will turn against her, warriors will be dismayed, and the wisdom of Edom will be destroyed.

- **The accusation — standing by while Jerusalem burned (Obad 10–14):** The heart of the charge: *"On the day that you stood aloof, on the day that strangers carried off his wealth and foreigners entered his gates... you were like one of them."* Edom did not merely watch — it cut off the fugitives, handed survivors over to their enemies, gloated at the disaster, and looted the city in Jerusalem's hour of collapse.

- **The reversal — as you have done, it shall be done to you (Obad 15):** *"As you have done, it shall be done to you; your deeds shall return on your own head."* The Day of the LORD is near for all nations; the standard applied to Israel will be applied to Edom without exception.

- **The promise — Jacob will possess Esau's possessions (Obad 17–21):** On Mount Zion there shall be those who escape, and the house of Jacob shall possess those who dispossessed them. The book closes with one of the most compressed eschatological declarations in the prophets: *"The kingdom shall be the LORD's."*

---

### Main Characters

**Edom** — The nation descended from Esau, brother of Jacob; their shared ancestry makes the betrayal more damning than ordinary geopolitical hostility. **Israel** — The house of Jacob, the victim of Edom's betrayal, whose restoration is the final word of the book.

---

### Jerusalem

The entire book is a response to Jerusalem's fall. Edom's crimes are enumerated specifically in relation to what happened to Jerusalem — standing aloof while it burned, looting its wealth, blocking the escape of its survivors. Jerusalem's desolation is the occasion of the prophecy; Jerusalem's restoration is its conclusion.

---

### Connection to Jesus

Obadiah's closing promise — *"the kingdom shall be the LORD's"* — is part of the larger prophetic stream announcing God's ultimate reign, which Jesus inaugurates with *"the kingdom of God is at hand."* The judgment of Edom for its treatment of Jacob's descendants also belongs to the pattern of Matthew 25:40 — what nations do to God's people, they do to him.
