# Daniel

**Overview:** Daniel and three companions are taken from Jerusalem to Babylon as young men in 605 BC and spend their careers navigating the court of a foreign empire without surrendering their identity or faith. The first half of the book is narrative — court tales of courage and supernatural deliverance. The second half is apocalyptic — Daniel receives visions spanning the arc of world history from Babylon to the end of the age. The book is eschatologically foundational: its imagery, numbers, and title *"Son of Man"* run directly into the New Testament.

---

### Key Moments

- **Food and Faithfulness (Dan 1):** Daniel and his friends refuse the king's food — almost certainly a matter of kosher law and refusal to participate in food sacrificed to idols — and propose a ten-day vegetable diet as a test. They emerge healthier than the others. The first and smallest act of resistance establishes the book's theme: fidelity to God in the heart of empire is not only possible but vindicated.

- **Nebuchadnezzar's Statue Dream (Dan 2):** The king dreams of a colossal statue — gold head, silver chest, bronze torso, iron legs, clay-and-iron feet — struck by a stone cut without human hands. The stone becomes a mountain filling the earth. Daniel interprets the metals as successive world empires; the stone is a kingdom God himself will establish that will never be destroyed. The dream spans human history and ends with divine interruption.

- **The Fiery Furnace (Dan 3):** Nebuchadnezzar builds a golden statue and demands universal worship. Shadrach, Meshach, and Abednego refuse. He throws them into a furnace heated seven times hotter than normal. The king looks in and sees four men walking unbound — *"the fourth is like a son of the gods."* They emerge unharmed, without even the smell of smoke. The identity of the fourth figure has been debated from the patristic period to the present.

- **Nebuchadnezzar's Humiliation (Dan 4):** Nebuchadnezzar dreams of a great tree cut down, its stump bound with iron. Daniel interprets it as judgment on the king's pride. It happens: the king is driven from human society to live as an animal for seven years — eating grass, his hair growing like eagle feathers, his nails like bird claws. His sanity returns when he acknowledges that *"the Most High rules the kingdom of men."*

- **The Writing on the Wall (Dan 5):** Nebuchadnezzar's son Belshazzar throws a feast using the sacred vessels looted from Jerusalem's Temple. A hand appears and writes on the wall: *"Mene, mene, tekel, upharsin."* Daniel is summoned and reads the verdict: *"You have been weighed in the balances and found wanting."* Belshazzar is killed that night; Darius the Mede takes the kingdom.

- **Daniel in the Lions' Den (Dan 6):** Political rivals engineer a law against praying to anyone but the king, knowing Daniel will not comply. He prays at his window three times a day as always. He is thrown to the lions. The den is sealed with the king's signet. In the morning Darius rushes to find Daniel unharmed: *"My God sent his angel and shut the lions' mouths."*

- **The Four Beasts and the Ancient of Days (Dan 7):** Daniel's most theologically significant vision. Four beasts rise from the sea — lion, bear, leopard, a terrifying fourth beast with ten horns. Then: *"thrones were placed, and the Ancient of Days took his seat"* — white hair, fiery throne, ten thousand times ten thousand attendants. The beasts are stripped of power. *"And behold, with the clouds of heaven there came one like a son of man, and he came to the Ancient of Days and was presented before him. And to him was given dominion and glory and a kingdom, that all peoples, nations, and languages should serve him; his dominion is an everlasting dominion."* This is the passage that provides the title Jesus claims for himself.

- **The Seventy Weeks (Dan 9):** Daniel, praying over Jeremiah's prophecy of the seventy-year exile, receives the vision of seventy *weeks* (units of seven years) — a timeline for Jerusalem, its Temple, and the arrival of *"an anointed one."* The prophecy is one of the most analyzed and debated in the OT; its terminus has been read christologically, pointing to the Messiah, from the NT period onward.

- **The Final Vision (Dan 10–12):** A heavenly messenger describes a great conflict among angelic powers over the nations, a time of trouble *"such as never has been since there was a nation,"* the resurrection of the dead — *"some to everlasting life, and some to shame and everlasting contempt"* — and the sealing of the book until the time of the end.

### Main Characters

**Daniel** — Jewish exile, interpreter of dreams, visionary; the consummate wise man who refuses compromise without making his refusals theatrical. His prayers, especially in Dan 9, are among the most theologically dense in the OT. **Shadrach (Hananiah), Meshach (Mishael), Abednego (Azariah)** — Daniel's three companions; their Babylonian names are given alongside their Hebrew names throughout, a small persistent signal of their dual existence. **Nebuchadnezzar** — The great Babylonian king whose arc in the book moves from arrogance to a genuine doxology of the Most High (Dan 4:34–37) — the only Gentile king in the OT who ends by worshipping Israel's God. **Belshazzar** — His son, who does not learn from his father's humiliation and desecrates the Temple vessels; he is killed in the night of the writing on the wall. **Darius the Mede** — The king under whom Daniel faces the lions' den; historically uncertain, perhaps a title or appointee rather than an independent monarch.

---

### Jerusalem

The seventy weeks prophecy (Dan 9) begins with Daniel praying over Jerusalem's desolation and receives a divine response explicitly structured around the city and its Temple: *"Seventy weeks are decreed about your people and your holy city."* The timeline runs to *"the anointed one"* and includes a future desolation of the Temple — the *"abomination of desolation"* that Jesus references directly in the Olivet Discourse. Jerusalem is not merely the historical backstory; it is the prophetic geography around which Dan 9's entire timeline is organized.

---

### Connection to Jesus

The single most direct Old Testament source for Jesus's self-understanding is Daniel 7:13. *"One like a son of man"* who comes on the clouds to the Ancient of Days and receives an everlasting kingdom is the text Jesus quotes before the Sanhedrin: *"You will see the Son of Man seated at the right hand of Power and coming on the clouds of heaven"* (Matt 26:64; Mark 14:62). This is not allusion — it is direct quotation and explicit self-identification. *"Son of Man"* is Jesus's most frequent self-designation in the Gospels, and its meaning is unlocked entirely by Dan 7. The Olivet Discourse (Matt 24; Mark 13) draws on Daniel's language throughout — *"abomination of desolation"* (Dan 9:27; 11:31; 12:11), *"time of tribulation,"* the elect gathered by angels. The fourth figure in the furnace (*"like a son of the gods,"* Dan 3:25) has been read since the early Fathers as a pre-incarnate appearance of Christ.
