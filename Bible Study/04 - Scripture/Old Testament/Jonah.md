# Jonah

**Overview:** A prophet commanded to preach repentance to Nineveh, the capital of Israel's most feared enemy, who runs in the opposite direction, is swallowed by a great fish, and eventually goes — only to be furious when the mission succeeds. The book is as much a diagnosis of Jonah's heart as a record of Nineveh's repentance: God's mercy toward enemies is the very thing the prophet cannot accept.

---

### Key Moments

- **The commission and the flight (Jon 1:1–3):** God commands Jonah to go to Nineveh. Jonah boards a ship at Joppa bound for Tarshish — the opposite direction, as far west as east was required. The narrative emphasises the irony without comment.

- **The storm and the sailors (Jon 1:4–16):** God hurls a great wind onto the sea. The pagan sailors pray to their gods; Jonah is asleep below deck. When the lot falls on him, Jonah identifies himself and tells them to throw him overboard. They resist, exhaust every alternative, and finally comply. The storm stops instantly. The sailors — pagans — fear the LORD and offer vows. They respond to God more reverently than the prophet does.

- **Jonah's prayer from the fish (Jon 2):** Swallowed by a great fish, Jonah prays a psalm of thanksgiving — not a prayer for rescue, but gratitude as if the rescue has already happened. *"Salvation belongs to the LORD."* The fish vomits him onto dry land.

- **Nineveh repents (Jon 3):** Jonah enters the city and delivers eight words: *"Yet forty days, and Nineveh shall be overthrown."* The king of Nineveh hears, descends from his throne, covers himself in sackcloth, and issues a royal decree covering people and animals alike. God relents. It is the most effective sermon in Scripture — and Jonah hates the result.

- **Jonah's anger (Jon 4:1–4):** *"It displeased Jonah exceedingly, and he was angry."* He tells God this is why he fled — he knew God was gracious and merciful, slow to anger, abounding in steadfast love, and he did not want Nineveh to receive any of it. He asks to die.

- **The plant, the worm, and God's final question (Jon 4:5–11):** God appoints a plant to shade Jonah, then a worm to kill it, then a scorching east wind. Jonah grieves the plant. God's closing question: *"You pity the plant, for which you did not labour... Should I not pity Nineveh, that great city, in which there are more than 120,000 persons who do not know their right hand from their left, and also much cattle?"* The book ends on the question — Jonah's answer is never recorded.

---

### Main Characters

**Jonah** — An Israelite prophet (identified in 2 Kings 14:25) whose obedience is ultimately coerced, not willing. His second journey to Nineveh is technically compliant but his heart remains closed — which is the book's real subject. **The sailors** — Unnamed pagan mariners whose response to God's power is more genuinely reverent than the prophet's. **The king of Nineveh** — Acts without hesitation: hears the word, humbles himself publicly, extends the call to repentance across the entire city. **The great fish** — God's instrument of both judgment and preservation; not a monster but a transport.

---

### Connection to Jesus

Jesus cites Jonah directly as a sign of his own death and resurrection: *"no sign will be given to it except the sign of the prophet Jonah. For just as Jonah was three days and three nights in the belly of the great fish, so will the Son of Man be three days and three nights in the heart of the earth"* (Matt 12:39–40). He also uses Nineveh's repentance as a rebuke to his contemporaries: *"The men of Nineveh will rise up at the judgment with this generation and condemn it, for they repented at the preaching of Jonah, and behold, something greater than Jonah is here"* (Matt 12:41). Jesus is the greater Jonah — who goes willingly, whose three days in death accomplish what Jonah's three days only foreshadowed.
