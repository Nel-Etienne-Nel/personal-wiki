# Zechariah

**Overview:** The most messianic of the Minor Prophets — contemporary with [[Haggai]], active from 520 BC through the rebuilding of the Second Temple, but ranging far beyond it into eschatological vision. Eight night visions, two major oracles, and a density of prophetic imagery that the NT — especially the Passion narratives and Revelation — quotes more heavily per page than almost any other OT book. The book holds the immediate task of Temple rebuilding and the ultimate destiny of Jerusalem in the same frame.

---

### Key Moments

- **The eight night visions (Zech 1–6):** Received in a single night and covering the full scope of God's purposes: horses patrolling the earth (1), four horns and four craftsmen (1–2), a man with a measuring line going to measure Jerusalem (2), Joshua the high priest reclothed in clean garments as Satan is rebuked (3), the golden lampstand and two olive trees (4), a flying scroll of judgment (5), a woman in a basket representing wickedness removed from the land (5), four chariots going out to the four winds (6). Each vision is given an interpretation; together they form a narrative arc from present desolation to ultimate restoration.

- **Not by might, nor by power (Zech 4:6):** God's word to Zerubbabel during the lampstand vision: *"Not by might, nor by power, but by my Spirit, says the LORD of hosts."* The mountain of obstacles will become a plain. Zerubbabel's hands laid the foundation; his hands will complete it.

- **Joshua crowned as type of the Branch (Zech 6:9–15):** A crown is placed on Joshua the high priest's head and the oracle announces: *"Behold, the man whose name is the Branch: for he shall branch out from his place, and he shall build the temple of the LORD... He shall bear royal honour, and shall sit and rule on his throne. And there shall be a priest on his throne, and the counsel of peace shall be between them both."* A priest-king who builds the final Temple — an office that belongs to none of the returnees, but to the coming Messiah.

- **The king comes on a donkey (Zech 9:9):** *"Rejoice greatly, O daughter of Zion! Shout aloud, O daughter of Jerusalem! Behold, your king is coming to you; righteous and having salvation is he, humble and mounted on a donkey, on a colt, the foal of a donkey."* The announcement of a king who comes in meekness rather than military power — a deliberate reversal of every imperial entry.

- **Thirty pieces of silver (Zech 11:12–13):** Zechariah is told to ask for his wages as a shepherd and is paid thirty pieces of silver — *"the lordly price at which I was priced by them."* God tells him to throw it to the potter in the house of the LORD. He does. Matthew 27:9–10 identifies this as fulfilled in Judas's thirty pieces returned to the Temple and used to buy the potter's field.

- **The pierced one mourned (Zech 12:10):** *"And I will pour out on the house of David and the inhabitants of Jerusalem a spirit of grace and pleas for mercy, so that, when they look on me, on him whom they have pierced, they shall mourn for him, as one mourns for an only child."* Quoted in John 19:37 at the moment the soldier's spear pierces Jesus's side.

- **The scattered shepherd (Zech 13:7):** *"Strike the shepherd, and the sheep will be scattered."* Quoted by Jesus himself at Gethsemane (Matt 26:31) as the prophecy being fulfilled when he is taken and the disciples flee.

- **The Mount of Olives splits; the LORD reigns (Zech 14):** A final eschatological battle — Jerusalem surrounded, a great earthquake splitting the Mount of Olives, the LORD going out to fight, living waters flowing from Jerusalem, and a day when the LORD will be king over all the earth. *"The LORD will be one and his name one."*

---

### Main Characters

**Zechariah** — Son of Berechiah, son of Iddo; he both delivers practical encouragement for the rebuilding community and receives visions of world-historical scope. **Joshua (son of Jehozadak)** — The high priest who appears in the visions, reclothed and crowned as a messianic type. **Zerubbabel** — The governor whose hands will complete the Temple; he receives the *"not by might"* oracle personally.

---

### Jerusalem

Jerusalem appears over 40 times. The city's attack, God's dramatic defense of it, and the nations streaming to it for worship are the subject of the great oracles in Zechariah 12–14. Zechariah sees Jerusalem as the axis of eschatological history: it is where the nations gather in hostility and where they are ultimately defeated; it is from Jerusalem that living waters flow; it is to Jerusalem that all nations will come to keep the Feast of Booths. No other Minor Prophet gives Jerusalem this magnitude of attention.

---

### Connection to Jesus

Zechariah's fingerprints are all over the Passion narrative and the Gospels. The triumphal entry (Zech 9:9 cited in Matt 21:5 and John 12:15), the thirty pieces of silver (Zech 11:12 cited in Matt 27:9), the pierced one (Zech 12:10 cited in John 19:37 and Rev 1:7), the scattered shepherd (Zech 13:7 cited by Jesus in Matt 26:31), and the cleansing of the nations (Zech 13:1) — each Passion account reaches back into Zechariah to explain what is happening. Revelation's imagery of the throne, the seven lamps, the four horses, and the new Jerusalem all draw heavily on Zechariah's night visions. The priest-king figure of Zechariah 6 is the template for the Christology of Hebrews: Jesus as both high priest and Davidic king, the two offices united in the one Branch.
