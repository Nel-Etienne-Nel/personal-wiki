# Ruth

**Overview:** Set during the period of the judges — *"when the judges ruled"* (1:1) — Ruth is a short narrative of extraordinary density and grace, providing relief from the moral chaos of [[Judges]]. A Moabite widow, Ruth, refuses to leave her Israelite mother-in-law Naomi after both their husbands die and commits herself irrevocably to Naomi's people and God. She is redeemed by Boaz, a wealthy kinsman of Naomi, and their union produces Obed — the grandfather of David. The book insists that the covenant God of Israel draws in unexpected people from unexpected places.

---

### Key Moments

- **Ruth's Declaration to Naomi (Ruth 1:16–17):** When Naomi releases her daughters-in-law to return to their own families, Orpah leaves — reasonably — but Ruth clings to Naomi. *"Where you go I will go, and where you lodge I will lodge. Your people shall be my people, and your God my God. Where you die I will die, and there will I be buried."* This is one of the most complete expressions of covenant loyalty in the entire OT — spoken by a Gentile outsider.

- **Ruth Gleaning in Boaz's Field (Ruth 2):** Ruth works behind the harvesters in Boaz's field, exercising the right of the poor under Mosaic Law (Lev 19:9). Boaz has already heard about her loyalty to Naomi and instructs his workers to leave extra grain for her and protect her. Their first conversation is a study in hesed — covenant lovingkindness operating through ordinary social arrangements.

- **The Threshing Floor (Ruth 3):** Naomi instructs Ruth to go to the threshing floor at night, uncover Boaz's feet, and lie down — a culturally understood gesture of requesting marriage and redemption. Boaz recognizes the legal claim and praises Ruth for not chasing younger men. He agrees to act as kinsman-redeemer but discloses there is a nearer kinsman who has first right.

- **Boaz Redeems Ruth (Ruth 4):** Boaz meets the nearer kinsman at the city gate, the proper legal forum. The kinsman is willing to buy Naomi's land but balks when he learns it comes with Ruth and the obligation to carry on the dead husband's name. Boaz takes everything — the land, the widow, the name, the obligation. The sandal is exchanged; the deal is witnessed by the elders at the gate.

- **The Genealogy (Ruth 4:17–22):** *"They named him Obed. He was the father of Jesse, the father of David."* The book's final eight verses are its pivot point: a Gentile widow's faithfulness places her in the direct line of Israel's greatest king — and beyond.

---

### Main Characters

**Ruth** — A Moabite who has no legal or ethnic claim on Israel's covenant but whose loyalty and character exceed most Israelites in the book. She is the active center of the narrative; the book bears her name. **Naomi** — Bitter, depleted, and honest about it (*"do not call me Naomi; call me Mara"*, 1:20), she is the one whose situation drives the plot and whose redemption is the story's emotional endpoint. **Boaz** — A man of standing and integrity; his generosity toward Ruth precedes any romantic interest. He is an Israelite exemplifying the best of covenant life. **Orpah** — Naomi's other daughter-in-law, who makes the sensible and not blameworthy choice to return to her own people; she serves as a foil for Ruth's extraordinary commitment.

---

### Jerusalem

Boaz is a resident of Bethlehem (2:4), described as a man of standing from the clan of Elimelech. Bethlehem is where David will be born ([[1 Samuel]] 17:12) and, centuries later, where Jesus is born (Matt 2:1). Ruth's story is geographically rooted in the same small town that will produce the Davidic line — not an accident.

---

### Connection to Jesus

Ruth appears in Matthew 1's genealogy of Jesus — one of only five women listed, and a Gentile. Her inclusion alongside Tamar, Rahab, Bathsheba, and Mary is pointed: God works salvation through socially marginal women, through Gentile outsiders, through irregular circumstances. The ethnic boundary of Israel's covenant was always more porous than ethnic Israel assumed.

Boaz as kinsman-redeemer (Hebrew: go'el) is one of the most textured OT types for Christ. The go'el must be a relative — related to the one he redeems. He must be willing. He must have the means. He pays the price to restore what was lost, to carry the name of the dead, to bring the outside person into full belonging. Jesus, fully human and therefore our kinsman, redeems what was lost at a cost only he could bear, and brings those with no claim into the family of God.
