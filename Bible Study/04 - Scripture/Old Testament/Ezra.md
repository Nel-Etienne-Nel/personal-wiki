# Ezra

**Overview:** The return from Babylonian exile in two waves — first under Zerubbabel, who leads the people back and rebuilds the Temple (538–516 BC), then under Ezra the scribe, who arrives decades later to lead spiritual reformation (458 BC). The book is about more than physical return: it is about whether the reconstituted community will be genuinely different from the one that went into exile.

---

### Key Moments

- **Cyrus's decree; first return under Zerubbabel (Ezra 1–2):** Cyrus of Persia issues his decree — fulfilling Isaiah's prophecy by name (Isa 44:28) — and the first wave of exiles returns. The Temple vessels plundered by Nebuchadnezzar are counted and carried back. A list of returnees follows: approximately 50,000 people, organized by family. The counting matters — this is a second exodus.

- **The Temple foundation laid; old men weep (Ezra 3:12):** The altar is rebuilt and the Feast of Booths kept before the Temple is even begun. When the foundation is laid, the priests and Levites lead worship and the people shout with great joy — but the old men who had seen Solomon's Temple weep. The sound of joy and the sound of weeping cannot be distinguished from a distance. The new beginning is also a reminder of what was lost.

- **Opposition delays rebuilding for years (Ezra 4):** The neighboring peoples — the Samaritans and others — first offer to help build, then when refused, set out to frustrate the work by writing to the Persian court. They succeed in halting construction for years during the reigns of Artaxerxes and Ahasuerus. Discouragement and opposition are the context for the next moment.

- **Haggai and Zechariah prophesy; Temple completed (Ezra 5–6):** The prophets Haggai and Zechariah arrive and stir the people back to work. The governor and his officials challenge the building; the returned exiles appeal to Cyrus's original decree; Darius I has it found in the archives and orders not only that work continue but that the Persian treasury fund it. The Temple is completed in 516 BC and dedicated with great celebration. The Passover is kept for the first time since the return.

- **Ezra arrives with a second wave of returnees (Ezra 7–8):** Sixty years after the Temple's completion, Artaxerxes commissions Ezra — described as a scribe skilled in the Law of Moses — to go to Jerusalem with a royal grant, silver and gold, and authorization to appoint judges and teach the Law. Ezra's genealogy traces him directly to Aaron. He acknowledges that the journey itself is a test of God's protection, refusing to ask for a military escort because he had told the king that God's hand is on all who seek him.

- **Ezra's horror at the intermarriages; mass repentance (Ezra 9–10):** Ezra arrives to discover that Israelites — including priests and Levites — have intermarried with the peoples of the land. He tears his garment, pulls hair from his head and beard, and sits appalled until the evening offering. His public prayer of confession draws a crowd; a man named Shecaniah proposes a covenant: send the foreign wives and children away. The assembly weeps loudly. Over three months, the men are called before an investigative assembly and the intermarriages dissolved.

---

### Main Characters

**Cyrus** — the Persian king whose decree is the pivot of Israel's history; identified in Isaiah by name a century before his birth. **Zerubbabel** — the Davidic descendant who leads the first return and lays the Temple foundation; a figure of messianic expectation in [[Haggai]] and [[Zechariah]]. **Jeshua** — the high priest who serves alongside Zerubbabel; the priestly counterpart to his civil leadership. **Ezra** — priest and scribe; his defining characteristic is that he had set his heart to study the Law, to do it, and to teach it. His grief at the community's covenant failure is the grief of a man who takes Torah seriously.

---

### Jerusalem

The entire book is oriented around Jerusalem and its Temple. The city is the destination of every returning wave, the Temple the focus of every effort. Rebuilding the altar and then the Temple is not about reclaiming property — it is about reconstituting the place where God meets his people. The emotional weight of Ezra 3:12 (the weeping of the old men) shows how much Jerusalem's Temple means to those who remember it.

---

### Connection to Jesus

The rebuilt Second Temple in Ezra is the Temple Jesus will enter, teach in, cleanse, and predict the total destruction of. [[Nehemiah]] and Ezra together set the stage for the world of Second Temple Judaism into which Jesus is born. Ezra's identity as priest-scribe, his role as authoritative interpreter of Torah, and his leading of communal repentance make him a partial anticipation of the one who will come as both prophet and priest, bringing the definitive interpretation of the Law and the definitive forgiveness of sins.
