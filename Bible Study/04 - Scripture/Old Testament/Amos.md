# Amos

**Overview:** Amos is a shepherd and fig farmer from Tekoa in the southern kingdom of Judah, sent north to prophesy in Israel during the prosperous reign of Jeroboam II (c. 760 BC). He is not a professional prophet and says so. His message is unambiguous: economic exploitation, judicial corruption, and the prostitution of religious life for social stability have made Israel's worship an offense to God, and judgment is coming. Amos is the first prophet in the canonical order whose words were preserved as an independent book, and his demand that justice precede worship stands as one of the starkest prophetic claims in the OT.

---

### Key Moments

- **The Trap of the Opening Oracles (Amos 1–2):** Amos opens by pronouncing judgment on Israel's neighbors — Damascus, Gaza, Tyre, Edom, Ammon, Moab — and then on Judah. Each oracle follows the same formula: *"For three transgressions... and for four, I will not revoke the punishment."* To an Israelite audience this was enjoyable. Then the hammer falls: the longest and sharpest oracle is against Israel itself. The rhetorical trap closes.

- **The Indictment: Selling the Poor (Amos 2:6–7):** *"They sell the righteous for silver, and the needy for a pair of sandals — those who trample the head of the poor into the dust of the earth and turn aside the way of the afflicted."* And simultaneously: *"a man and his father go into the same girl, so that my holy name is profaned."* Economic exploitation and sexual immorality are both named in the same breath. The indictment is not abstract.

- **The Logic of Relationship (Amos 3):** *"Do two walk together unless they have agreed?"* — the rhetorical questions of Amos 3:3–6 build the logic: every effect has a cause. Then the pointed application: *"You only have I known of all the families of the earth; therefore I will punish you for all your iniquities."* Covenant privilege does not provide immunity from judgment — it intensifies accountability. This is one of the most important theological moves in the prophetic corpus.

- **God Hates Their Feasts (Amos 5:21–24):** *"I hate, I despise your feasts, and I take no delight in your solemn assemblies. Even though you offer me your burnt offerings and grain offerings, I will not accept them; and the peace offerings of your fattened animals, I will not look upon them. Take away from me the noise of your songs; to the melody of your harps I will not listen."* God does not need the worship of those who oppress the poor. Then the counterpoint: *"But let justice roll down like waters, and righteousness like an ever-flowing stream."* This is the most-quoted verse in the book and one of the most demanding in the OT.

- **The Vision of the Plumb Line (Amos 7:7–9):** God stands beside a wall with a plumb line and holds it against Israel. Israel does not measure up. The sanctuaries of Jeroboam will be destroyed. The image is precise: God is not applying an arbitrary standard but measuring against the standard the covenant itself established.

- **Amaziah vs. Amos (Amos 7:10–17):** The priest of Bethel sends word to King Jeroboam that Amos is conspiring against him and commands Amos to go back to Judah: *"earn your bread there, and prophesy there."* Amos's reply is one of the sharpest in all of prophecy: *"I was no prophet, nor a prophet's son, but I was a herdsman and a dresser of sycamore figs. But the LORD took me from following the flock, and the LORD said to me, 'Go, prophesy to my people Israel.'"* Then a direct personal oracle against Amaziah.

- **The Vision of the Summer Fruit (Amos 8):** God shows Amos a basket of summer fruit. The Hebrew word for summer fruit (*qayits*) sounds like the word for end (*qets*). *"The end has come upon my people Israel."* The wordplay is sharp; the vision is brief; the judgment is final.

- **The Promise of Restoration (Amos 9:11–14):** After unrelenting judgment, the book closes with a restoration oracle: *"In that day I will raise up the booth of David that is fallen... I will restore the fortunes of my people Israel... they shall plant vineyards and drink their wine, and they shall make gardens and eat their fruit."* The restoration is as specific and physical as the judgment was.

---

### Main Characters

**Amos** — Farmer-turned-prophet; his outsider status is part of his authority. He is not embedded in the religious establishment and owes nothing to it. His language is concrete and agrarian — lions, plumb lines, fruit baskets, sieves — and his moral reasoning is tight. **Amaziah** — The priest of Bethel, the royal sanctuary of the northern kingdom; he is the establishment's defender and serves as the foil that draws out Amos's clearest self-identification. His attempt to silence Amos fails; he receives a personal oracle of judgment. **Jeroboam II** — King of Israel during whose prosperous reign Amos prophesies; the prosperity is the problem, having produced inequality and the comfortable conflation of religious observance with political stability.

---

### Connection to Jesus

Amos 5:25–27 is quoted by Stephen in his defense before the Sanhedrin (Acts 7:42–43) as evidence that Israel's wilderness worship was already compromised — building a case for the pattern of rejecting God's messengers that climaxes in Jesus's execution. James quotes Amos 9:11–12 at the Jerusalem Council (Acts 15:16–17) — *"I will rebuild the tent of David that has fallen"* — as scriptural warrant for including Gentiles in the people of God without requiring circumcision. It is a pivotal use of a prophetic text to adjudicate a defining ecclesiological question. Jesus's cleansing of the Temple (*"you have made it a den of robbers"*, Matt 21:13) enacts exactly Amos's critique: a religious institution that has become a mechanism of social power, with the worship of God reduced to its cover.
