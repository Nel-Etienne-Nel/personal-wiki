# Micah

**Overview:** A prophet from the village of Moresheth in Judah's lowlands, contemporary with [[Isaiah]] and addressing both the northern and southern kingdoms during their final decades. Micah combines some of the sharpest social justice oracles in the prophets — aimed at corrupt rulers, greedy landlords, and prophets for hire — with some of the most luminous messianic promises, including a precise prophecy of the Messiah's birthplace.

---

### Key Moments

- **Judgment on Samaria and Jerusalem for corruption (Mic 1–3):** The oracle opens with God himself as witness coming down to tread the high places. The charges are concrete: leaders who hate good and love evil, who eat the flesh of God's people (Mic 3:1–3), and prophets who *"cry 'Peace' when they have something to eat, but declare war against him who puts nothing in their mouths"* (Mic 3:5). Micah names the consequence directly: *"Zion shall be plowed as a field; Jerusalem shall become a heap of ruins"* (Mic 3:12) — a prophecy cited a century later when Jeremiah faces execution (Jer 26:18).

- **The nations stream to the mountain of the LORD (Mic 4:1–4):** A vision of the last days where all nations come to Jerusalem to be taught, swords are beaten into ploughshares, and every man sits under his own vine and fig tree. This passage is nearly identical to [[Isaiah]] 2:2–4 — two contemporaries receiving the same vision.

- **The Bethlehem prophecy (Mic 5:2):** *"But you, O Bethlehem Ephrathah, who are too little to be among the clans of Judah, from you shall come forth for me one who is to be ruler in Israel, whose origin is from of old, from ancient days."* A ruler whose origins are eternal will come from an insignificant village. This is one of the most geographically and theologically specific messianic prophecies in the OT.

- **God's lawsuit against Israel (Mic 6:1–8):** God calls the mountains as witnesses and puts his case to his people. After recounting his acts of redemption, he asks what Israel owes him — and answers with the most compressed ethical summary in the prophets: *"He has told you, O man, what is good; and what does the LORD require of you but to do justice, and to love kindness, and to walk humbly with your God?"* (Mic 6:8).

- **The sins of the city catalogued (Mic 6:9–7:6):** Dishonest scales, violence, lies, bribes — the city's commercial and judicial life is systematically corrupt. Micah's lament in Mic 7:1–6 is among the most personally felt passages in the prophets: *"the faithful have vanished from the earth."*

- **The confession and the promise (Mic 7:7–20):** The book ends in trust and doxology. *"I will look to the LORD; I will wait for the God of my salvation."* The final verses reach climactic assurance: *"He will again have compassion on us; he will tread our iniquities underfoot. You will cast all our sins into the depths of the sea"* (Mic 7:19).

---

### Main Characters

**Micah** — A rural prophet, not a court prophet or temple official. His perspective is from the land: he knows what it costs ordinary people when leaders abuse power, and he does not soften the accusation. He also holds the longest view — beyond destruction to the Bethlehem ruler whose origin is from ancient days.

---

### Jerusalem

Jerusalem and its corrupt leadership are the direct target of Micah's most blistering oracles. The rulers build Zion with blood (Mic 3:10); the priests teach for price; the prophets divine for money — all while claiming God is among them and no disaster will come. Micah demolishes that assumption. His prophecy that Zion will be *"plowed as a field"* (Mic 3:12) is the most explicit prediction of Jerusalem's destruction before it happened, and it was accurate. Jerusalem also appears at the book's centre as the future gathering point of the nations (Mic 4), holding judgment and hope in tension.

---

### Connection to Jesus

Micah 5:2 is the text the chief priests and scribes cite when Herod asks where the Messiah is to be born (Matt 2:4–6). It is the direct answer to a direct question, and it is from Micah — not from [[Isaiah]] or the Psalms. The verse names Bethlehem by name, identifies the ruler as one of primordial origin, and places his coming from a place of insignificance — all of which the Nativity narrative fulfils point by point. Micah 7:6 (*"a man's enemies are the men of his own house"*) is quoted by Jesus in Matthew 10:35–36 to describe the division his coming brings.
