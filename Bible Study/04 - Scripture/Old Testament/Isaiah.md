# Isaiah

**Overview:** The greatest of the writing prophets, ministering in Jerusalem across the reigns of Uzziah, Jotham, Ahaz, and Hezekiah (roughly 740–700 BC). The book addresses an immediate political crisis (the Assyrian threat), an anticipated catastrophe (Babylonian exile, 150 years away), the return from that exile, and finally a cosmic horizon — new creation. Isaiah contains more direct messianic prophecy than any other Old Testament book, and the New Testament cites it more than any other prophet.

---

### Key Moments

- **The Call Vision (Isa 6):** In the year King Uzziah died, Isaiah sees the LORD enthroned, attended by seraphim crying *"Holy, holy, holy is the LORD of hosts; the whole earth is full of his glory."* The temple shakes; Isaiah is undone by his own uncleanness. A seraph touches his lips with a burning coal: *"Your guilt is taken away, and your sin atoned for."* Then the commission: *"Whom shall I send?"* — *"Here I am. Send me."* The pattern of vision, undoing, cleansing, and commissioning shapes the whole prophetic vocation.

- **The Sign of Immanuel (Isa 7:14):** During the Syro-Ephraimite crisis, Isaiah offers Ahaz a sign — *"Therefore the LORD himself will give you a sign. Behold, the virgin shall conceive and bear a son, and shall call his name Immanuel."* The sign has an immediate historical dimension and a horizon the NT identifies as Jesus's birth (Matt 1:23).

- **The Child Born to Us (Isa 9:6–7):** *"For to us a child is born, to us a son is given; and the government shall be upon his shoulder, and his name shall be called Wonderful Counselor, Mighty God, Everlasting Father, Prince of Peace."* A throne name with divine titles — the Davidic king who will bring endless peace.

- **The Shoot from the Stump of Jesse (Isa 11):** After judgment falls on Assyria, a new Davidic king emerges from the cut-down dynasty — the Spirit of the LORD rests on him, he judges with righteousness, and the wolf lies down with the lamb. Creation itself is restored under his rule.

- **Comfort, Comfort My People (Isa 40):** The hinge of the book — exile assumed as accomplished, the word shifts to consolation. *"Comfort, comfort my people, says your God."* A voice crying in the wilderness: prepare the way of the LORD. *"He will tend his flock like a shepherd."* The nations are like a drop from a bucket; the LORD does not grow faint or weary.

- **The Servant Songs (Isa 42, 49, 50, 52–53):** Four poems about a figure called the Servant of the LORD. He is chosen, Spirit-anointed, a light to the nations, who suffers willingly on behalf of others. The fourth song (Isa 52:13–53:12) is the most precise: *"He was pierced for our transgressions; he was crushed for our iniquities; upon him was the chastisement that brought us peace, and with his wounds we are healed."* Substitutionary, vicarious suffering.

- **Cyrus Named as Deliverer (Isa 44:28–45:1):** God names Cyrus — by name — as the king who will authorize the rebuilding of Jerusalem and the Temple, roughly 150 years before Cyrus was born. *"He is my shepherd, and he shall fulfill all my purpose."* One of the most specific predictive prophecies in Scripture.

- **The Invitation (Isa 55):** *"Come, everyone who thirsts, come to the waters; and he who has no money, come, buy and eat!"* Free grace — not earned, not purchased. *"Seek the LORD while he may be found."* The word of God that goes out will not return to him empty.

- **New Heavens and New Earth (Isa 65:17–25):** The book ends not with return from exile but with cosmic renewal: *"For behold, I create new heavens and a new earth, and the former things shall not be remembered."* Weeping is gone; infant mortality is gone; the wolf and the lamb feed together. Revelation 21 draws on this directly.

### Main Characters

**Isaiah son of Amoz** — A Jerusalem prophet with apparent access to the court; married (his wife is called "the prophetess"), with at least two sons whose names are signs (Shear-jashub: "a remnant shall return"; Maher-shalal-hash-baz: "swift spoil, speedy plunder"). **Ahaz** — King of Judah during the Syro-Ephraimite crisis; offered a sign by God and refuses to ask, preferring Assyrian alliance to trust. **Hezekiah** — The faithful king who receives Isaiah's support during Sennacherib's siege; also rebuked for showing Babylon his treasury. **Sennacherib** — The Assyrian king whose 185,000 troops die outside Jerusalem (Isa 36–37). **The Servant** — A figure who is sometimes Israel collectively, sometimes an individual within Israel, sometimes a single person whose vocation transcends Israel — the ambiguity is intentional and resolved in Jesus. **Cyrus** — The Persian king prophesied by name as God's "anointed" (45:1) who will release the exiles.

---

### Jerusalem

Isaiah is a Jerusalem prophet through and through — the city is not backdrop but subject. The book opens with indictment of Jerusalem's corruption (Isa 1), moves through its threatened destruction, its miraculous deliverance under Hezekiah (Isa 36–37), its coming desolation under Babylon (Isa 39), and its ultimate restoration. Isaiah 40–66 addresses the community of exiles with promises of return to the city. The suffering servant's work results in the vindication of Zion (Isa 54). The new Jerusalem of the closing chapters (Isa 60–62, 65–66) is not merely the old city rebuilt but a city transformed — *"the LORD will be your everlasting light."*

---

### Connection to Jesus

Isaiah is the most cited Old Testament book in the New Testament. Jesus opens his public ministry by reading Isaiah 61 in the Nazareth synagogue — *"Today this Scripture is fulfilled in your hearing"* (Luke 4:21). John the Baptist is explicitly identified as the voice of Isaiah 40:3 in all four gospels. Matthew applies the Immanuel sign (Isa 7:14) and the great light (Isa 9:1–2) to Jesus's birth and early ministry. The suffering servant of Isaiah 53 is applied to Jesus in every gospel and explicitly expounded by Philip to the Ethiopian eunuch (Acts 8:32–35). Peter quotes Isaiah 53:5 in the context of Jesus's atoning death (1 Pet 2:24). Paul's mission to the Gentiles is framed by Isaiah 49:6 (*"I will make you a light for the nations"*). Hebrews, Romans, and Revelation all draw heavily on Isaiah's framework. The book anticipates Jesus not as a single proof-text but as a sustained theological architecture — servant, king, light, shepherd, redeemer, creator of the new heavens and earth.
