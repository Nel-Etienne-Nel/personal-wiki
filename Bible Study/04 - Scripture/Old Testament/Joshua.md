# Joshua

**Overview:** Israel enters and conquers Canaan under Joshua's leadership, fulfilling the land promise God made to Abraham in [[Genesis]] 12 and 15. The book moves through military campaigns, miraculous interventions, covenant renewals, and the tribal division of the land. Yet even at the end, the conquest is incomplete — pockets of Canaanites remain, and the seeds of future compromise are already visible.

---

### Key Moments

- **Rahab and the Scarlet Cord (Josh 2):** Two Israelite spies are hidden by Rahab, a Canaanite prostitute in Jericho, who confesses Israel's God is the true God. She extracts a promise of safety; they mark her window with a scarlet cord. Her household alone survives the destruction of Jericho.

- **Crossing the Jordan (Josh 3):** As the priests carrying the Ark step into the flooded Jordan, the waters cut off upstream and the entire nation crosses on dry ground — a deliberate echo of the Red Sea crossing in [[Exodus]]. Twelve stones are set up as a memorial.

- **The Fall of Jericho (Josh 6):** Israel marches around the city once a day for six days, then seven times on the seventh day. At the priests' trumpet blast and the people's shout, the walls collapse. The city is placed under the ban (herem) — total destruction devoted to God.

- **Achan's Sin and the Defeat at Ai (Josh 7):** One man, Achan, secretly takes plunder from Jericho in violation of the ban. Israel loses the next battle at Ai. Achan is identified by lot, confesses, and is executed along with his household. The principle is stark: hidden covenant-breaking has communal consequences.

- **The Gibeonite Deception (Josh 9):** The Gibeonites, terrified of Israel, dress as travelers from a distant land and trick Israel into a peace treaty. Joshua makes the treaty without consulting God. He honors it when the truth emerges — they become hewers of wood and drawers of water — but the failure of discernment haunts later generations.

- **The Sun Stands Still (Josh 10):** During the southern campaign, Joshua prays and the sun stops over Gibeon while Israel pursues their enemies. *"There has been no day like it before or since, when the LORD heeded the voice of a man"* (10:14).

- **Division of the Land (Josh 13–21):** The latter half of the book methodically distributes the conquered territory among the twelve tribes. Cities of refuge are established. The Levites receive towns rather than territory.

- **Joshua's Final Address and Covenant Renewal at Shechem (Josh 24):** Joshua gathers all Israel, recites God's acts from Abraham to the present, and calls the people to choose whom they will serve. *"As for me and my house, we will serve the LORD"* (24:15). The covenant is renewed; Joshua dies at 110.

---

### Main Characters

**Joshua** — Moses's successor, a proven military commander and man of faith who was one of only two spies to bring a faithful report from Canaan forty years earlier. **Rahab** — A Canaanite prostitute whose faith in Israel's God saves her household and places her in the lineage of David and Jesus. **Caleb** — The other faithful spy; at 85, still claims his mountain portion and drives out the Anakim. **Achan** — The Israelite whose private disobedience demonstrates that covenant violation cannot be contained individually.

---

### Jerusalem

Jerusalem appears in Joshua as Jebus — still in Canaanite hands. The Jebusites are not expelled: *"But the people of Judah could not drive out the Jebusites, the inhabitants of Jerusalem, so the Jebusites dwell with the people of Judah at Jerusalem to this day"* (15:63). The city will remain unconquered until David takes it in [[2 Samuel]] 5 and makes it his capital — the arc from Joshua's incomplete conquest to David's fulfillment spans several centuries.

---

### Connection to Jesus

Joshua's Hebrew name is Yeshua — etymologically identical to the Greek Iesous (Jesus), meaning "the LORD saves." The name connection is not incidental: both lead God's people into an inheritance through the death of enemies; both require crossing a body of water as the threshold of a new era.

[[Hebrews]] 4:8 makes the typology explicit: *"For if Joshua had given them rest, God would not have spoken of another day later on."* Joshua's conquest and rest in Canaan was a true but provisional fulfillment — a physical land pointing to the eschatological rest that Jesus provides. Rahab's scarlet cord, hung in the window as the sign of her salvation amid judgment, has long been read as a type of the blood that marks those who are passed over.
