# Deuteronomy

**Overview:** Moses's farewell address delivered to the new generation of Israel on the plains of Moab, poised to enter Canaan — the generation whose parents died in the wilderness. It is not a simple repetition of [[Leviticus]] and [[Numbers]] but a pastoral retelling: Moses preaches the Law, urging Israel to love God and obey, and sets before them life or death, blessing or curse. The book ends with Moses's death on Mount Nebo, having seen the land but never entered it.

---

### Key Moments

- **The Shema (Deut 6:4–5):** *"Hear, O Israel: The LORD our God, the LORD is one. You shall love the LORD your God with all your heart and with all your soul and with all your might."* The foundational confession of Israel's monotheism and the command Jesus calls the greatest commandment.

- **Blessings and Curses (Deut 28):** An extended catalogue of the covenant's consequences — extraordinary national flourishing if Israel obeys, devastating judgment (exile, siege, disease, scattering) if it rebels. Israel's later history reads as a commentary on this chapter.

- **Prediction of Exile and Return (Deut 30):** Moses prophesies that Israel will break the covenant, be scattered among the nations, and yet God will circumcise their hearts and restore them. *"The LORD your God will circumcise your heart and the heart of your offspring, so that you will love the LORD your God with all your heart"* (30:6) — new-covenant language inside the old covenant.

- **Moses's Song (Deut 32):** A poetic witness against Israel's future rebellion, composed by Moses and taught to the people. It recounts God's faithfulness, predicts Israel's unfaithfulness, and declares God's ultimate vindication.

- **The Prophet Like Moses (Deut 18:15):** Moses promises that God will raise up a prophet from among Israel's brothers *"like me"* — one who speaks God's words directly. Israel is to listen to him.

- **Joshua Appointed (Deut 31):** Moses publicly commissions Joshua before all Israel, laying hands on him and charging him with the conquest. God himself confirms Joshua in the tent of meeting.

- **Moses Views the Land and Dies (Deut 34):** God shows Moses the entire Promised Land from the top of Mount Nebo — from Gilead to the western sea. Moses dies there at 120 years, his eyes undimmed. *"There has not arisen a prophet since in Israel like Moses, whom the LORD knew face to face"* (34:10). He is buried by God in an unknown grave.

---

### Main Characters

**Moses** — The mediator of the Sinai covenant, now aged 120, who cannot enter the land because of his striking the rock at Meribah. The entire book is his voice. **Joshua** — Moses's appointed successor, already proven in battle and in faith; he will lead the crossing and the conquest (see [[Joshua]]).

---

### Jerusalem

Deuteronomy 12 commands Israel to worship only at *"the place the LORD your God will choose out of all your tribes to put his name and make his habitation there."* The place is unnamed but is consistently understood in later Scripture as Jerusalem — particularly the Temple Mount. This phrase becomes a recurring anchor in Deuteronomy (12:5, 11, 14, 18, 21, 26) that shapes all of Israel's subsequent theology of centralized worship.

---

### Connection to Jesus

During his wilderness temptation (Matt 4 / Luke 4), Satan tests Jesus three times. Each time, Jesus responds with a direct quotation from Deuteronomy: *"Man shall not live by bread alone"* (Deut 8:3), *"You shall not put the LORD your God to the test"* (Deut 6:16), and *"You shall worship the LORD your God, and him only shall you serve"* (Deut 6:13). Jesus, the true Israel, succeeds where Israel failed — and does so through the words Moses spoke to the old Israel.

Moses's promise of a coming prophet — *"The LORD your God will raise up for you a prophet like me from among you, from your brothers — it is to him you shall listen"* (18:15) — is explicitly applied to Jesus by Peter (Acts 3:22) and Stephen (Acts 7:37). The pattern of Moses is significant: mediator of the covenant, intercessor for the people, the one through whom God speaks. Jesus is the greater Moses, mediating a new and better covenant ([[Hebrews]] 3:1–6).
