# Proverbs

**Overview:** Practical wisdom for navigating daily life — speech, work, money, sex, children, friendship, and the fear of God. Mostly attributed to Solomon, though the book explicitly contains multiple collections from different hands and periods. Wisdom here is not abstract philosophy; it is skill at living in alignment with the way God made the world.

---

### Key Moments

- **The Prologue (Prov 1–9) — Wisdom as a Woman:** Extended poems framing the whole collection. A father instructs his son. Two women call out from the streets: Lady Wisdom, who builds a house and sets a feast, and Lady Folly, who leads men to their deaths. The choice between them is the choice that shapes everything else.

- **Wisdom at Creation (Prov 8) — The Cosmic Craftsman:** Wisdom speaks in the first person about being present at creation — *"When he established the heavens, I was there... I was beside him, like a master workman."* This is the most theologically dense passage in the book: wisdom is not just useful, it is woven into the structure of reality.

- **The Thesis (Prov 9:10):** *"The fear of the LORD is the beginning of wisdom, and knowledge of the Holy One is understanding."* The entire book unfolds from this premise — wisdom is not neutral competence but a relational posture toward God.

- **The Proverbs of Solomon (Prov 10–22):** The main body — hundreds of individual, often antithetical sayings set in couplets. Righteous vs. wicked, wise vs. foolish, diligent vs. lazy, humble vs. proud. They reward slow reading and resist being systematized.

- **Hezekiah's Collection (Prov 25–29):** *"These also are proverbs of Solomon, which the men of Hezekiah king of Judah copied."* A reminder that the book had a long editorial history — kings and scribes shaped what was preserved.

- **The Words of Agur (Prov 30):** A confession of ignorance that sharpens into theological precision: *"Who has ascended to heaven and come down? Who has gathered the wind in his fists?... What is his name, and what is his son's name? Surely you know!"* One of the most striking rhetorical questions in the OT.

- **The Virtuous Woman / Eshet Chayil (Prov 31:10–31):** An acrostic poem — each verse begins with a successive Hebrew letter — praising a woman of strength, industry, generosity, and wisdom. The poem closes the book deliberately: a life shaped by Lady Wisdom looks like this in practice.

---

### Main Characters

**Solomon** — Named as the primary teacher and the dominant voice throughout. His failure to live by his own wisdom (see [[1 Kings]]) gives the collection a tragic undertone. **Lady Wisdom** — A personified figure who speaks, builds, feasts, and calls; she is not merely metaphor — she has cosmic standing (Prov 8). **Lady Folly** — Wisdom's counterpart; seductive, loud, and lethal. **Lemuel's Mother** — The unnamed woman whose words to her king-son constitute Proverbs 31:1–9, followed by the Eshet Chayil. **Agur son of Jakeh** — Author of Proverbs 30; his identity is unknown, his rhetoric unforgettable.

---

### Connection to Jesus

Proverbs 8's portrayal of Wisdom — present at creation, the agent through whom the world was made — is the direct conceptual background for John 1:1 (*"In the beginning was the Word"*) and Colossians 1:15–17 (*"He is the image of the invisible God... all things were created through him"*). Paul explicitly names Jesus *"the wisdom of God"* (1 Cor 1:24, 30). The Wisdom literature as a whole shapes the NT's Christology: Jesus does not merely teach wisdom, he is its embodiment and source. The contrast between wisdom's house and folly's house in Prov 9 maps onto the two builders parable in Matthew 7.
