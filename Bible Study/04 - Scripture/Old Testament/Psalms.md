# Psalms

**Overview:** The prayer book and hymnal of ancient Israel — 150 songs, laments, praises, and meditations assembled over centuries and used in Temple worship. They cover the full range of human experience before God: raw grief, fierce anger, confusion, repentance, wonder, and exaltation. The Psalter is not a theology textbook; it is Israel talking to God, honestly, and it models what that looks like.

---

### Key Moments

- **Psalm 1 — The Entry Psalm:** The righteous man meditates on God's law day and night and is *"like a tree planted by streams of water"* — the gateway psalm that frames the whole Psalter as a book of wisdom and instruction.

- **Psalm 22 — Forsakenness and Vindication:** Opens with *"My God, my God, why have you forsaken me?"* — a lament that maps with startling precision onto the crucifixion: mocking crowds, pierced hands and feet, garments divided by lot. It ends in vindication and global praise, not despair.

- **Psalm 23 — The Shepherd Psalm:** *"The LORD is my shepherd; I shall not want."* Six verses moving from green pastures through the valley of the shadow of death to the house of the LORD. One of the most memorized passages in Scripture.

- **Psalm 46 — God as Fortress:** *"God is our refuge and strength, a very present help in trouble."* A Zion psalm celebrating God's protection of the city against the nations. Luther's basis for "A Mighty Fortress."

- **Psalm 51 — The Deepest Penitential Psalm:** David's prayer after Nathan confronted him over Bathsheba and Uriah. *"Create in me a clean heart, O God, and renew a right spirit within me."* The most honest psalm of contrition in the Bible — it does not minimize, explain, or deflect.

- **Psalm 90 — Moses on Time and Mortality:** *"A thousand years in your sight are but as yesterday when it is past."* The oldest psalm, attributed to Moses — a stark meditation on human transience against God's eternity.

- **Psalm 110 — The Priest-King:** *"The LORD says to my Lord: 'Sit at my right hand, until I make your enemies your footstool.'"* The most quoted Old Testament verse in the New Testament. The figure addressed is a king from Zion and simultaneously a priest *"after the order of Melchizedek"* — a category that transcends the Levitical priesthood.

- **Psalm 119 — The Torah Psalm:** The longest chapter in the Bible — 176 verses in 22 sections, each section keyed to a letter of the Hebrew alphabet. A sustained meditation on God's word as the source of life, guidance, and delight. Every verse references the word in some form.

- **Songs of Ascent (Psalms 120–134):** Fifteen psalms sung by pilgrims walking up to Jerusalem for the three great festivals. They move from distress in a foreign land (Ps 120) to arrival and blessing at the Temple (Ps 134). *"I lift up my eyes to the hills — where does my help come from?"* (Ps 121).

- **Psalm 150 — The Closing Doxology:** Every instrument, every breath, every creature — *"Let everything that has breath praise the LORD."* The Psalter ends not in lament but in total, orchestral praise.

### Main Characters

**David** — Primary author; roughly half the psalms carry his name. Warrior, king, musician, adulterer, murderer, repentant — his range is the Psalter's range. **Asaph** — Temple musician and seer; his psalms (50, 73–83) wrestle especially with the prosperity of the wicked and God's justice. **Sons of Korah** — A guild of Temple musicians; their psalms (42–49, 84–85, 87–88) include some of the most beautiful expressions of longing for God's presence. **Solomon** — Attributed Psalms 72 and 127. **Moses** — Psalm 90, the oldest psalm in the collection. **Ethan the Ezrahite** — Psalm 89, a sustained lament over the apparent failure of the Davidic covenant.

---

### Jerusalem

Jerusalem and Zion are woven into the Psalter's structure. The Songs of Ascent (Ps 120–134) are physically about walking to the city — pilgrimage psalms designed for the journey up to the Temple. The Zion Psalms (46, 48, 76, 87) celebrate God's choice of Zion as his dwelling place and the city's inviolability. The Temple is the spatial center of the Psalms' world — the place where heaven and earth meet, where God's presence is localized, where the worshiper longs to be. *"How lovely is your dwelling place, O LORD of hosts!"* (Ps 84:1).

---

### Connection to Jesus

Psalms is the most cited Old Testament book in the New Testament by a wide margin. Jesus quotes Psalm 22:1 from the cross and Psalm 110:1 in the Temple to stump the Pharisees. At the Last Supper, the Hallel psalms (113–118) were sung. The NT explicitly treats Psalms 2, 16, 22, 45, 69, 72, 110, and 118 as messianic — not as allegory imposed from outside but as texts David wrote by the Spirit about one greater than himself. Peter's Pentecost sermon (Acts 2) centers on Psalm 16 and 110. Hebrews draws extensively on Psalms 2, 40, 95, 110 to establish who Jesus is. The Psalter is the hymnal of the early church before the church had written its own hymns.
