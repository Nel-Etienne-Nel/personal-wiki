# Malachi

**Overview:** The last prophet before 400 years of prophetic silence. Writing to the second Temple community — returned from exile but cold, cynical, and ritually corrupt — Malachi uses a series of disputation dialogues in which God states a charge and the people push back with *"How?"* He closes the Old Testament with the promise of a coming messenger, a coming refining judgment, and the return of Elijah — then the silence falls, and does not break until John the Baptist appears in the wilderness.

---

### Key Moments

- **God's love challenged (Mal 1:2–5):** God opens with *"I have loved you."* The people respond: *"How have you loved us?"* God answers with the election of Jacob over Esau — not sentiment but sovereign, historic, demonstrated love. The disputation pattern that structures the whole book is established here.

- **Corrupt priests indicted (Mal 1:6–2:9):** Priests are offering blind, lame, and sick animals on the altar — animals they would not offer to a governor, let alone to God. *"Oh that there were one among you who would shut the doors, that you might not kindle fire on my altar in vain!"* (Mal 1:10). God is not impressed by frequency of ritual when quality and heart are absent. The indictment extends to priests who have caused many to stumble by their corrupt instruction of the law (Mal 2:8).

- **Faithlessness in marriage condemned (Mal 2:10–16):** Men are divorcing the wives of their youth to marry foreign women. *"The LORD was witness between you and the wife of your youth, to whom you have been faithless, though she is your companion and your wife by covenant."* The verse *"the LORD hates divorce"* (Mal 2:16) sits in this context of covenant faithlessness, not a general prohibition extracted from its social setting.

- **The messenger who will prepare the way (Mal 3:1):** *"Behold, I send my messenger, and he will prepare the way before me. And the Lord whom you seek will suddenly come to his temple; and the messenger of the covenant in whom you delight, behold, he is coming, says the LORD of hosts."* Two comings in one verse: a forerunner and the Lord himself. The question that follows is unsettling: *"But who can endure the day of his coming, and who can stand when he appears?"* The answer implies: not those who think they want it.

- **The tithe and the test (Mal 3:8–12):** *"Will man rob God? Yet you are robbing me."* God invites — uniquely in Scripture — testing him: *"Bring the full tithe into the storehouse... and thereby put me to the test, says the LORD of hosts, if I will not open the windows of heaven for you and pour down for you a blessing until there is no more need."*

- **The book of remembrance (Mal 3:16–18):** Those who feared the LORD spoke with one another; God listened and a book of remembrance was written. *"They shall be mine, says the LORD of hosts, in the day when I make up my treasured possession."* A remnant is preserved even in a corrupt generation.

- **Elijah promised before the great and awesome day (Mal 4:5–6):** *"Behold, I will send you Elijah the prophet before the great and awesome day of the LORD comes. And he will turn the hearts of fathers to their children and the hearts of children to their fathers, lest I come and strike the land with a decree of utter destruction."* The final word of the OT is not merely a promise — it is a warning hung on the promise. The silence that follows lasts four centuries.

---

### Main Characters

**Malachi** — The name means *"my messenger,"* which may be a personal name or a description; either way it is fitting, since the book is largely structured as a series of divine messages delivered to a resistant audience. **The priests of Jerusalem** — Not villainous outsiders but insiders who have grown careless and cynical in their sacred responsibilities; their failure is the corruption at the centre of the community's spiritual decline.

---

### Jerusalem

The Temple and its worship are the central concern of the book. The priests are officiating in Jerusalem; the altar is in Jerusalem; the messenger of the covenant will come suddenly to *"his temple"* in Jerusalem. Malachi's prophetic ministry is entirely focused on the second Temple community's failure to honour God in the place and manner he has prescribed.

---

### Connection to Jesus

Malachi 3:1 — *"Behold, I send my messenger to prepare the way before me"* — is applied to John the Baptist by Jesus himself: *"This is he of whom it is written, 'Behold, I send my messenger before your face, who will prepare your way before you'"* (Matt 11:10). Malachi 4:5 — the promise of Elijah's return — is confirmed by Jesus as fulfilled in John: *"And if you are willing to accept it, he is Elijah who is to come"* (Matt 11:14), and again after the Transfiguration: *"Elijah has already come, and they did not recognise him"* (Matt 17:12). The angel Gabriel explicitly frames John's mission in Malachi's terms (Luke 1:17). Malachi is the OT book that most literally anticipates the opening chapter of the Gospels — the forerunner, the sudden coming of the Lord, the refining fire — and the silence after it is precisely the gap the Incarnation breaks.
