# Song of Solomon

**Overview:** An erotic love poem celebrating human romantic love without apology — desire, longing, pursuit, union, and the fierce jealousy of genuine love. The woman's voice dominates and initiates, which is unusual in ancient Near Eastern literature. It has been read both literally and allegorically (God's love for Israel; Christ's love for the church) since antiquity, and the two readings are not mutually exclusive: the book's literal reality is what makes the allegory mean anything.

---

### Key Moments

- **The Woman's Opening Desire (Song 1:2–4):** *"Let him kiss me with the kisses of his mouth! For your love is better than wine."* The woman speaks first and expresses desire directly — the poem begins with her voice, not his.

- **The Banqueting House (Song 2:4):** *"He brought me to the banqueting house, and his banner over me was love."* An image of public declaration and protective belonging — love as both shelter and proclamation.

- **Mutual Possession (Song 2:16, 6:3):** *"I am my beloved's and my beloved is mine."* The formula of mutual belonging — repeated with slight variations across the poem — is its emotional center. The relationship is marked by full reciprocity.

- **The Lover's Approach (Song 2:8–13):** *"The voice of my beloved! Behold, he comes, leaping over the mountains..."* The winter is past, the rain is over, the flowers appear — his coming is described in terms of the whole creation waking up.

- **The Nightmare of Loss (Song 3:1–4, 5:2–8):** Twice the woman searches for her beloved and cannot find him. The second time she is found by the watchmen and beaten. These passages acknowledge that love includes the terror of potential loss — the poem is not naive.

- **Love Strong as Death (Song 8:6–7):** *"Set me as a seal upon your heart, as a seal upon your arm, for love is strong as death, jealousy fierce as the grave. Its flashes are flashes of fire, the very flame of the LORD. Many waters cannot quench love, neither can floods drown it."* The climax of the poem. Love here is not sentiment — it is a force comparable to death itself, and it originates in God (*shalhevetyah* — "flame of the LORD").

---

### Main Characters

**The Beloved Woman** — The primary voice and protagonist; dark-skinned (Song 1:5), beautiful, bold, unashamed. She drives the poem's action. **The Lover** — Described in extravagant physical terms (Song 5:10–16); traditionally identified with Solomon, though the poem also has pastoral imagery (shepherd, vineyards) that complicates a purely royal reading. **The Daughters of Jerusalem** — A chorus who interact with the woman throughout; they receive her descriptions of her beloved and witness her longing.

---

### Jerusalem

The daughters of Jerusalem appear throughout the poem as a kind of Greek chorus, and the poem is set against a backdrop of a royal court connected to the city. Jerusalem is named directly in Songs 1:5, 2:7, 3:5, 3:10, 5:8, 5:16, 6:4, 8:4. The city is context and witness — the lovers' relationship exists within a community, not in isolation.

---

### Connection to Jesus

The book entered the Jewish canon in part because Rabbi Akiva called it *"the holy of holies"* of Scripture — he read it as Israel's love relationship with God. The early church extended this: the lover is Christ, the beloved is the church. Paul's *"Christ loved the church and gave himself up for her... that she might be holy and without blemish"* (Eph 5:25–27) draws on exactly this imagery. The church as the bride of Christ — developed in Ephesians 5, Revelation 19 and 21 — is the Song's allegorical register extended to its fullest. The literal poem is not erased by this reading; rather, human love is shown to be patterned after something even deeper.
