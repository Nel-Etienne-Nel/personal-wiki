# Nahum

**Overview:** A prophecy of judgment against Nineveh — the same city that repented under [[Jonah]]. Roughly a century has passed; Nineveh is now at the height of Assyrian imperial power, and its cruelty has returned without restraint. Nahum announces its total destruction with a graphic specificity that is deliberately uncomfortable: this is an unqualified celebration of a brutal empire's end, and the book does not apologise for it.

---

### Key Moments

- **God's character as the ground for judgment (Nah 1:2–8):** The book opens with a theological statement before a single Assyrian crime is named: *"The LORD is a jealous and avenging God; the LORD is avenging and wrathful; the LORD takes vengeance on his adversaries... slow to anger and great in power, and the LORD will by no means clear the guilty."* The attributes usually cited for comfort — patience, power — are here the very reason Nineveh cannot escape.

- **Good news for Jerusalem (Nah 1:15):** The pivot from theophany to promise: *"Behold, upon the mountains, the feet of him who brings good news, who publishes peace! Keep your feasts, O Judah; fulfill your vows, for never again shall the worthless pass through you; he is utterly cut off."* Nineveh's destruction is Jerusalem's deliverance. This verse is echoed in Isaiah 52:7 and cited in Romans 10:15.

- **The siege and sack of Nineveh described (Nah 2):** The battle scene is vivid and immediate — charging cavalry, flashing swords, the river gates thrown open, the palace in terror, silver and gold plundered. The city that looted nations is itself looted. *"Nineveh is like a pool whose waters run away."*

- **The indictment of the bloody city (Nah 3:1–7):** *"Woe to the bloody city, all full of lies and plunder — no end to the prey!"* The description of chariot charges, heaped corpses, and Nineveh's prostituted sorceries is not hyperbolic: Assyrian records confirm the systematic terror. The humiliation announced is calibrated to Nineveh's own practices.

- **The final verdict (Nah 3:18–19):** Addressing the king of Assyria directly: *"Your shepherds are asleep... your people are scattered on the mountains with none to gather them. There is no easing your hurt; your wound is grievous. All who hear the news about you clap their hands over you."* The book closes with the nations applauding — because Nineveh's fall is the end of its victims' suffering, not merely its own.

---

### Main Characters

**Nineveh** — The city itself is the protagonist and target; no human characters are named. The unnamed Assyrian king addressed in Nah 3:18 is a figure of collapsed authority.

---

### Jerusalem

Nineveh's destruction is explicitly framed as good news for Jerusalem. The verse that forms the emotional turning point of the book (Nah 1:15) speaks directly to Judah: keep your feasts, fulfill your vows — the oppressor is gone. Jerusalem's freedom from Assyrian threat is the positive face of every judgment oracle in the book. Nahum prophesied during a period when Nineveh was the most powerful city in the world; declaring its imminent destruction was itself a statement of faith on Jerusalem's behalf.
