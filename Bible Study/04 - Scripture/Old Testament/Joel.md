# Joel

**Overview:** A locust plague of catastrophic scale — stripping the land bare, silencing the grain offering, cutting off the wine for libation — becomes in Joel's hands the launching pad for the most developed early treatment of the *"Day of the LORD"* in the OT. The book moves from agricultural crisis to cosmic judgment to the outpouring of the Spirit on all flesh, and closes with the restoration of Judah and the judgment of the nations. Joel is short but theologically dense, and its Pentecost prophecy makes it one of the most consequential texts in the entire canon.

---

### Key Moments

- **The Locust Plague (Joel 1):** The book opens in extremis: a locust invasion without precedent, described in four waves (cutting, swarming, hopping, destroying locusts), has stripped every vine and fig tree and silenced the grain offering and drink offering in the Temple. Joel calls the elders, the priests, and the whole people to lament — *"the fields are ruined, the ground mourns."* The disaster is not merely economic; it is liturgical. The Temple's worship has been interrupted.

- **The Call to Repentance (Joel 2:12–13):** *"Yet even now,"* — before the greater Day arrives — *"return to me with all your heart, with fasting, with weeping, and with mourning; and rend your hearts and not your garments."* The call is corporate and urgent. Joel appeals not to Israel's performance but to God's character: *"for he is gracious and merciful, slow to anger, and abounding in steadfast love."* This formulation echoes Exodus 34:6 — the great self-disclosure at Sinai — and is the theological spine on which the call rests.

- **The Promise of the Spirit (Joel 2:28–29):** *"And it shall come to pass afterward, that I will pour out my Spirit on all flesh; your sons and daughters shall prophesy, your old men shall dream dreams, and your young men shall see visions. Even on the male and female servants in those days I will pour out my Spirit."* The democratisation of prophetic gift across gender, age, and social class is the promise: the Spirit, previously given to particular individuals for particular tasks, will be poured out without restriction.

- **Signs and the Day of the LORD (Joel 2:30–31):** Blood and fire and columns of smoke, the sun darkened, the moon turned to blood *"before the great and awesome day of the LORD comes."* The cosmic signs frame the Day as something that interrupts the natural order. Joel 2:32 then pivots: *"And it shall come to pass that everyone who calls on the name of the LORD shall be saved."* Judgment and salvation are both in view simultaneously.

- **Judgment in the Valley of Jehoshaphat (Joel 3):** The nations are assembled in the Valley of Jehoshaphat — a valley whose name means *"the LORD judges"* — for reckoning. The specific charge against them is scattering God's people and dividing the land (Joel 3:2). The harvest metaphor: *"Put in the sickle, for the harvest is ripe."* The vision is eschatological and total.

- **Restoration (Joel 3:18):** After judgment: *"And in that day the mountains shall drip sweet wine, and the hills shall flow with milk, and all the streambeds of Judah shall flow with water; and a fountain shall come forth from the house of the LORD and water the Valley of Shittim."* The locust-stripped land is reversed into abundance. The spring from the Temple echoes the life-giving water in [[Ezekiel]] 47.

---

### Main Characters

**Joel** — Identified only as *"son of Pethuel"*; the date of his ministry is debated (anywhere from the ninth to the fourth century BC). He is addressed to Jerusalem and Judah; he knows the Temple liturgy intimately — the grain offering, the drink offering, the blowing of the trumpet in Zion — and his vision is structured around the restoration of proper worship. **The Locust Army** — Not a character in the conventional sense, but a presence that dominates the entire first movement of the book. Whether the army in Joel 2 is still the locusts read apocalyptically, or a human army arriving after the locusts, is a long-standing interpretive question.

---

### Jerusalem

Joel is addressed specifically to Jerusalem and Judah; the Temple and its interrupted worship are the immediate context for the entire book. The blowing of the trumpet in Zion (Joel 2:1, 15), the weeping between the vestibule and the altar (Joel 2:17), the restoration of the grain offering in the house of the LORD (Joel 2:14) — the Temple frames the whole. The Valley of Jehoshaphat where the nations are judged is traditionally located adjacent to Jerusalem. The spring from the Temple in Joel 3:18 recapitulates the city's centrality in the restoration.

---

### Connection to Jesus

Peter's sermon at Pentecost (Acts 2) opens with the explicit identification: *"this is what was uttered through the prophet Joel"* (Acts 2:16), then quotes Joel 2:28–32 in full. The outpouring of the Spirit on the disciples — men and women, young and old — is the direct fulfillment of Joel's promise. Peter applies Joel 2:32 — *"everyone who calls on the name of the LORD shall be saved"* — to Jesus: the name that must be called upon is his (Acts 2:21, 36). Paul quotes the same verse in Romans 10:13 as the foundation of his gospel call to Gentiles. Jesus's apocalyptic language in the Olivet Discourse draws on Joel's cosmic signs — sun darkened, moon not giving its light (Matt 24:29; cf. Joel 2:31). The spring from the Temple (Joel 3:18) is one of the streams running through Jesus's promise of living water (John 7:37–38) and into Revelation 22.
