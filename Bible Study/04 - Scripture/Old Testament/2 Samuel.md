# 2 Samuel

**Overview:** David's full reign over a united Israel — his political and military triumphs, the establishment of Jerusalem as his capital, the bringing of the Ark to the city, and the Davidic covenant. Then the fracture: David's adultery with Bathsheba and the arranged murder of Uriah unleash consequences that tear his family apart and nearly destroy his kingdom. The book is a masterwork of theological history — David's greatness and failure held together without softening either.

---

### Key Moments

- **David Anointed King Over All Israel (2 Sam 2–5):** After [[1 Samuel]] ends with Saul's death, David is first anointed king over Judah alone in Hebron. A civil war between David's forces and the remnants of Saul's house, led by Abner and Ish-bosheth, lasts seven years. After Ish-bosheth's assassination by his own men, all Israel comes to Hebron and anoints David king. He has waited approximately fifteen years since Samuel's private anointing.

- **David Captures Jerusalem (2 Sam 5):** The Jebusites hold the city and mock David's advance — the blind and lame could defend it, they say. David takes it through the water shaft; the city becomes his. He names it the City of David. The city unconquered in [[Joshua]] 15:63 finally falls.

- **The Ark Brought to Jerusalem (2 Sam 6):** David brings the Ark up to Jerusalem with great celebration — until Uzzah touches the Ark to steady it and dies instantly. David is frightened and leaves the Ark with Obed-edom for three months. When he sees Obed-edom's household blessed, he brings it the rest of the way with sacrifice and dancing. David dances before the LORD with abandon; his wife Michal despises him for it. She remains barren to her death.

- **The Davidic Covenant (2 Sam 7):** David wants to build God a house. God refuses — David is a man of war — but promises instead to build David a house (a dynasty). *"Your house and your kingdom shall be made sure forever before me. Your throne shall be established forever"* (7:16). This is the central theological pivot of the entire OT. Every subsequent reference to a coming king, the Messiah, the son of David, flows from this chapter.

- **David and Bathsheba; Uriah's Murder (2 Sam 11):** David sees Bathsheba bathing from his roof and sends for her. She becomes pregnant. David arranges for her husband Uriah, a loyal soldier at the front, to come home — hoping he will sleep with his wife and obscure the paternity. Uriah refuses to sleep at home while his comrades are in the field. David sends Uriah back with sealed orders placing him at the front of the hardest fighting. Uriah is killed. David takes Bathsheba as his wife. *"But the thing that David had done displeased the LORD"* (11:27).

- **Nathan's Confrontation (2 Sam 12):** The prophet Nathan tells David a parable about a rich man who steals a poor man's only lamb. David's anger burns. *"You are the man."* Nathan lays out the consequences: the sword will never leave David's house; his wives will be violated publicly; the child of the adultery will die. David confesses immediately — *"I have sinned against the LORD."* Nathan confirms his life is spared, but the consequences stand. The baby dies. David rises, bathes, and worships God. His response confounds his servants.

- **Mephibosheth (2 Sam 9):** David asks if any of Saul's household remains, for Jonathan's sake. He finds Mephibosheth, Jonathan's crippled son, living obscurely in Lo-debar. David restores to him all of Saul's land and seats him at the royal table permanently — *"like one of the king's sons"* (9:11). An act of pure hesed, the living out of his covenant with Jonathan.

- **Absalom's Rebellion (2 Sam 15–18):** Absalom, David's son, nurses a grievance over David's failure to punish the rape of his sister Tamar. He cultivates popular support for years, then stages a coup. David flees Jerusalem — barefoot, weeping, with loyal men — while Absalom enters the city. David refuses to use the Ark as a talisman; he sends Zadok back with it. The rebellion is crushed; Absalom, riding a mule, gets his hair caught in a tree and is killed by Joab while hanging there — against David's explicit orders. *"O my son Absalom, my son, my son Absalom! Would I had died instead of you, O Absalom, my son, my son!"* (18:33).

### Main Characters

**David** — At his best: a man who forgives enemies, keeps covenants, dances before God, and runs to God in his worst moment. At his worst: a man who uses power to take what he wants and eliminate the evidence. Both are fully present. **Bathsheba** — Victim and then queen; largely passive in 2 Samuel but becomes decisive in [[1 Kings]] 1. **Uriah the Hittite** — A Gentile soldier of impeccable honor whose integrity inadvertently seals his own death sentence. His loyalty is a silent rebuke to David throughout chapter 11. **Nathan** — The prophet who serves David faithfully and holds him accountable; he confronts without flattery and offers forgiveness without softening the consequences. **Joab** — David's loyal but violent commander; he kills Abner (ch. 3), kills Absalom (ch. 18), and generally does David's dirty work. He is irreplaceable and ungovernable. **Absalom** — Beautiful, politically gifted, deeply wronged, and ultimately driven by bitterness into rebellion. His death is one of the most sorrowful passages in the OT. **Mephibosheth** — Jonathan's son, crippled in both feet; his redemption by David is a pure act of covenant loyalty unearned and unexpected.

---

### Jerusalem

2 Samuel is the founding moment of Jerusalem as the heart of Israel. David takes the city (ch. 5), brings the Ark there (ch. 6), and receives the promise of an eternal kingdom centered there (ch. 7). When Absalom rebels, David's departure from the city and return to it frames the crisis. Every subsequent OT use of Jerusalem — as the place of the Temple, the city of God, the site of God's presence — traces directly to what happens in 2 Samuel. The city is not incidental geography; it becomes a theological category.

---

### Connection to Jesus

2 Samuel 7 — the Davidic covenant — is the direct textual root of NT messianism. Gabriel's announcement to Mary quotes it almost verbatim: *"He will be great and will be called the Son of the Most High. And the Lord God will give to him the throne of his father David, and he will reign over the house of Jacob forever, and of his kingdom there will be no end"* (Luke 1:32–33; cf. 2 Sam 7:13–16). The promise God makes to David — that his son will build the Temple, that God will be his father and he his son, that his kingdom will endure forever — is given a partial, provisional fulfillment in Solomon, but its full weight requires a Son who literally does not die. Paul cites 2 Sam 7:14 in 2 Cor 6:18 and the author of [[Hebrews]] cites it in Heb 1:5 as uniquely applicable to Jesus. David's pattern of rejected-then-enthroned king, who rescues the outcast and redeems what was lost, runs through the NT's portrait of Jesus from baptism to resurrection.
