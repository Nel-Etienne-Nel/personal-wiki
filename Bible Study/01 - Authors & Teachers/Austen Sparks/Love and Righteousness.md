# Love and Righteousness

[[Austin Sparks]] | Sources: *Abounding in Love*, *All Righteousness Fulfilled*

---

## Abounding in Love — The Mark of the End Times

The epistles addressing Christ's return — Thessalonians and John — place love above all else. Paul prays that the Thessalonians' love "may abound" (1 Thess. 3:12), then later gives thanks that it does (2 Thess. 1:3). This is the quality that draws Christ's heart; love is the magnetic characteristic as the end approaches.

Sparks makes a sharp distinction: "Knowledge puffeth up, but love buildeth up" (1 Cor. 8:1). The priestly function in Scripture represents the heart-love dimension; the kingly represents administration. God desires genuine heart devotion over perfect theological formulation. This is not anti-intellectual — it is a priority ordering.

The Samuel and David illustration (1 Sam. 16:7): "Man looketh on the outward appearance, but the Lord looketh on the heart." Offensive behaviours or doctrinal deficiencies in other believers should not prevent finding authentic spiritual devotion beneath the surface. Withdrawing into critical judgment paralyses ministry. Love creates possibilities where criticism sees only failure. Real spiritual work requires pursuing connection through love rather than maintaining critical distance.

The warning is implicit: it is possible to be theologically correct and spiritually inert. Love is what builds; love is what Christ is looking for.

Key references: 1 Thess. 3:12; 2 Thess. 1:3; Eph. 1:15; 4:16; 1 John 3:22–23; 4:7; 1 Cor. 8:1; 1 Sam. 16:7; Mal. 3:16–17.

## All Righteousness Fulfilled — The Settled Question

Christ's baptism and crucifixion represent a once-and-for-all fulfilment of righteousness before God. This is not provisional or temporary; it is final.

Sin as intrusion: sin is a foreign element that invaded God's kingdom. Since sin and the sinner are inseparable, unrighteous persons have no legitimate place in God's presence and must be judged through death. Righteousness is primarily about what God is due — it concerns His nature and His satisfaction, not merely our benefit.

When Christ died, He "put out all unrighteousness" and satisfied God's demand for righteous judgment once for all (cf. Heb. 10). Believers need never repeat this transaction through repeated baptisms or cycles of self-condemnation. What matters is not instant sinlessness but heart-posture: rejecting the old life and cleaving to Christ. That inward relationship — not perfect performance — establishes standing before God.

The evidence that this is genuinely believed: praise. Genuine praise flows from settled confidence that the righteousness question is resolved. When believers embrace this, they gain the joy and authority to overcome spiritual opposition (cf. 2 Chr. 20:22 — the singers went before the army).

Key references: Matt. 3:13–15; Heb. 2:12; 2 Chr. 20:22.

---

## How These Two Connect

They address the two foundational questions of the Christian life: *How do I stand before God?* (Righteousness — settled, finished, once for all) and *How do I live among others?* (Love — abounding, building, looking to the heart). Together they form the vertical and horizontal of Christian existence. Sparks roots both in Christ: righteousness is *in* Him, love is the fruit of knowing Him.

→ [[All Things in Christ]] — righteousness and love, like sanctification and the kingdom, are not independent virtues to chase; they are expressions of Christ Himself.
→ [[Austin Sparks]] — love connects back to the core thesis: genuine devotion to the Person, not a system of theology.
