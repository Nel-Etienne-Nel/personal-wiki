# The Ark of the Testimony

[[Austin Sparks]] | Source: *The Ark of the Testimony*

---

## The Three Elements

The ark of the covenant, in Sparks' reading, is a type of Christ in His greatness and glory as God perceives Him — "the greatness and the glory of the Lord Jesus as in the eye of God" (1 Chr. 13:1–14). The cherubim flanking it are custodians of what is most precious to God.

Three elements in the drama:

| Element | Type |
|---|---|
| The Ark | The testimony of Christ's supremacy; God's perspective on Christ |
| David | The instrument God raises to bring that testimony toward its final, settled resting place |
| The Philistines | The uncircumcised opposition — "the putting away of the body of the flesh" (Col. 2:11) resisted |

David's life's culmination was establishing a settled resting place for the ark — *finality*, not continual journeying. The testimony of Christ is meant to arrive, not merely to travel.

---

## The Philistine Principle — Resistance to the Cross

"Uncircumcised Philistines" represent spiritual opposition to the principle of complete separation unto God. Three historical failures illuminate the principle:

- **Samson**: Loss of Nazirite vows — representing absolute consecration — led directly to Philistine victory. Half-consecration is no consecration.
- **David's temptation**: Accepting Saul's armour would have meant adopting fleshly methods. He rejected it — the testimony cannot be carried by natural strength.
- **The new cart error**: Using mechanical means (a new cart) instead of consecrated priestly shoulders brought judgment through Uzza's death. The manner of carrying the testimony matters as much as the intention to carry it.

---

## The Principle

God requires that the instruments bearing His testimony maintain absolute separation from fleshly principles. The ark cannot be placed on a cart designed for transport convenience; it must be borne on consecrated shoulders, at personal cost.

Half-measures invite judgment. The testimony of Christ's supremacy requires carriers who have genuinely come to the Cross — Col. 2:11 being the entry point.
