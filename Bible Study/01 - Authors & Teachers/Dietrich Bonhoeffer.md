# **The Cost of Discipleship**



That's the book, specifically the opening chapter simply titled **"Costly Grace."** It's where he makes the famous cheap grace vs costly grace distinction.

The core of his argument is this: cheap grace is grace as a _doctrine_, a _principle_, a _system_ — grace that requires no discipleship, no cross, no change. You receive forgiveness without repentance, baptism without church discipline, communion without confession. Grace as a standing theological comfort rather than a living encounter that costs you something.

Costly grace, by contrast, is the grace that _calls you to follow_ — and in following, to die to yourself. He frames the whole thing around Jesus's words: _"whoever would save his life will lose it, but whoever loses his life for my sake will find it"_ (Matthew 16:25).

Bonhoeffer wrote it in Nazi Germany in the 1930s, watching the German church capitulate to Hitler partly because they had a theology that made comfort and cultural belonging feel spiritually acceptable. Cheap grace was not just a theological error to him — it was a _lethal_ one. He eventually died for the costly version of it.
