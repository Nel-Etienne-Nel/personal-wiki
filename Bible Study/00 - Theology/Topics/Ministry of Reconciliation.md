# Ministry of Reconciliation — 2 Corinthians 5:11–21

## The Core Text

The phrase itself appears in verse 18: *"All this is from God, who through Christ reconciled us to himself and gave us the ministry of reconciliation."* Then verse 20 draws out the implication: *"Therefore, we are ambassadors for Christ, God making his appeal through us. We implore you on behalf of Christ, be reconciled to God."*

---

## What Does Reconciliation (καταλλαγή — *katallagē*) Mean?

The Greek root carries the idea of **exchanging enmity for peace** — restoring a broken relationship to a state of favour. It's relational, not merely legal. Paul isn't just talking about the cancellation of a debt, but the restoration of a *person-to-person* relationship between humanity and God.

The word group (*katallassō / apokatallassō / katallagē*) is used almost exclusively by Paul in the NT, and almost always with **God as the initiating subject**. God is not the one who needed to be reconciled — we are. The alienation was on humanity's side (hostility, estrangement, rebellion), and God moved toward us in Christ to close that gap.

This is the heartbeat of the passage: **God was in Christ, reconciling the world to himself** (v.19).

---

## The Theological Structure of the Passage (vv. 14–21)

### 1. The Death of Christ as the Hinge of History (vv. 14–15)
The love of Christ "controls" (*synechei* — compels, constrains) Paul. Christ died *for all*, which means in some sense all died. The purpose: that those who live should no longer live for themselves but for him.

### 2. A New Way of Knowing (vv. 16–17)
Because of this, Paul no longer regards anyone "according to the flesh" — even Christ. The old categories of evaluation are gone. *"If anyone is in Christ, he is a new creation."* The Greek is striking here — *kainē ktisis* — new creation. Not just a renewed individual, but a cosmic category shift. The old order has passed; new things have come.

### 3. The Source and Content of Reconciliation (vv. 18–19)
*All of this is from God.* Reconciliation originates entirely in God's initiative. He accomplished it *through Christ*, and the content of it is this: **he did not count their trespasses against them.** This non-imputation is then explained by v. 21 — Christ was made sin so that we might become the righteousness of God *in him.*

### 4. The Ambassadorial Commission (v. 20)
Paul sees himself (and by extension, all gospel ministers) as **ambassadors** (*presbeuomen*). An ambassador doesn't represent his own agenda — he carries the message of the one who sent him, with the authority of that sender. The appeal Paul makes is not his own: *God is making his appeal through us.* The ministry of reconciliation is God continuing to reach toward estranged humanity through human messengers.

---

## The Great Exchange — v. 21

> *"For our sake he made him to be sin who knew no sin, so that in him we might become the righteousness of God."*

This verse sits at the theological centre of the whole passage and has historically been a key text for **Penal Substitutionary Atonement**, though it also touches on **recapitulation** and **union with Christ**.

- The one who *knew no sin* becomes sin — this is substitution.
- We become *the righteousness of God in him* — this is more than imputation; it is union. "In him" (*en autō*) is the locating phrase. Our righteousness is not a quality we possess independently; it exists *in Christ*.

The righteousness Paul describes is **participatory, not merely forensic** — directly relevant to the Chinese church's emphasis on union with Christ (Nee/Lee).

---

## What the "Ministry" Actually Involves

Paul uses *diakonia* (ministry/service) — servant language, not priestly office language. The content of the ministry is the **word of reconciliation** (v. 19), with two movements:

- **Proclamation**: declaring what God has done in Christ
- **Appeal**: *"Be reconciled to God."* An ongoing invitation — even to those who have already received the gospel, Paul continues urging them toward its full reality

Note the broader context: 2 Corinthians is deeply personal, and Paul is partly defending his apostolic ministry against critics in Corinth. The ministry of reconciliation isn't just a theological category; it's the *description of what Paul's suffering and preaching have been about the whole time.*

---

## Christ-Centrality

Reading through the lens of *how does this point to Jesus Christ?* — the answer here is that it barely points away from him. Christ is not the instrument of a doctrine; he is the **content and location** of reconciliation itself. The new creation isn't a program; it's found "in Christ." The righteousness isn't an abstract moral standard; it's the righteousness of God as it exists *in him.*

> This is a key passage for testing Austin-Sparks's warning: you could teach "reconciliation" as a doctrine and miss that Paul is describing an ongoing, relational reality that only exists through personal union with a living Person.
