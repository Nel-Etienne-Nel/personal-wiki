
Cheap grace is not just grace without repentance. It's more specifically **grace that has been disconnected from the living Person who gives it.** Repentance is the symptom of that disconnection, not the cause.

Here's the distinction that matters:

**Cheap grace** treats forgiveness as a _product_ you can access without a genuine encounter with Christ. You know the doctrine, you prayed a prayer, you're covered — and life continues largely unchanged. Repentance feels unnecessary because sin doesn't register as all that serious. God is gracious, after all.

**Costly grace** is what happens when you've actually encountered _who_ is forgiving you. When you've stood, even briefly, in the reality of what the cross actually was — the weight of it, the horror of it, the love _and_ the holiness of it — repentance isn't a requirement you reluctantly fulfill. It becomes an almost involuntary response. Like Peter saying _"depart from me, I am a sinful man"_ — nobody told him to say that. Proximity to holiness produced it naturally.

So the formula isn't really:

> Grace + Repentance = Costly Grace

It's more like:

> Real encounter with Christ → produces repentance → which is inseparable from receiving grace

**Repentance is the evidence that the grace was _real_** — that it landed on someone who actually grasped what they were being forgiven of and who was doing the forgiving. Where there's no repentance at all, ever, it's reasonable to ask whether genuine grace was received or just a comfortable idea about grace.

This connects directly back to the fear of the Lord conversation. Fear of the Lord and genuine repentance come from the same root — a true reckoning with who God is. Lose the fear, and repentance quietly disappears with it.
