##### John 1:1-18

In the beginning was the Word, and the Word was with God, and the Word was God. 2He was with God in the beginning. 3Through him all things were made; without him nothing was made that has been made. 4In him was life, and that life was the light of all mankind. 5The light shines in the darkness, and the darkness has not overcome it.

6There was a man sent from God whose name was John. 7*He came as a witness to testify concerning that light, so that through him all might believe*. 8He himself was not the light; he came only as a witness to the light.

9The true light that gives light to everyone was coming into the world. 10He was in the world, and though the world was made through him, the world did not recognize him. 11He came to that which was his own, but his own did not receive him. 12Yet to all who did receive him, to those who believed in his name, he gave the right to become children of God— 13children born not of natural descent, nor of human decision or a husband’s will, but born of God.

14The Word became flesh and made his dwelling among us. We have seen his glory, the glory of the one and only Son, who came from the Father, full of grace and truth.

15(John testified concerning him. He cried out, saying, “This is the one I spoke about when I said, ‘He who comes after me has surpassed me because he was before me.’ ”) 16Out of his fullness we have all received grace in place of grace already given. 17For the law was given through Moses; grace and truth came through Jesus Christ. 18No one has ever seen God, but the one and only Son, who is himself God and is in closest relationship with the Father, has made him known.
 

"He" is the Word — which John immediately identifies as Jesus Christ.
 

John opens his Gospel by deliberately echoing Genesis 1:1 ("In the beginning..."), and his whole point in these opening verses is to establish that Jesus didn't begin at Bethlehem. He existed eternally with the Father before creation itself.

Then in verse 14 John makes it explicit: "The Word became flesh and made his dwelling among us" — that's the incarnation. The eternal Word took on human form in Jesus.

What makes John 1:1 so theologically rich is the triple statement about the Word:

He was in the beginning — eternal existence. He was with God — distinct from the Father, a real relationship. He was God — fully divine in nature.

So "He" is the pre-incarnate Jesus Christ, the second person of the Trinity, who was present and active with the Father from eternity — and through whom everything was created. This directly connects to Colossians 1:16 and Hebrews 1:2, which both affirm that creation happened through the Son.

It's a stunning opening to the Gospel — John wants you to know before you read a single miracle or teaching: this Jesus you're about to encounter is not merely a great teacher. He is God himself, stepped into creation He made.

## Joy — Hebrews 12:2, Luke 10:21, John 15:11, John 17:13, Luke 15:1–7

### Another Aspect of Joy — Delight in the Lord

#### Key Passages: Psalm 37:4, Isaiah 42:1, John 4:34, Luke 10:21, Psalm 16:11, Isaiah 55:2, Isaiah 58:14

Joy in Jesus is not just emotional happiness or celebration — one of its deepest expressions is **delight**: a posture of settled, luxuriant pleasure in God Himself. Jesus is both the supreme *model* of this delight and, ultimately, its *object*.

---

#### What the Word Actually Means

The Hebrew word in Psalm 37:4 is **עָנַג** (*anag*) — it carries the sense of being soft, pliable, tender toward something. It is the word used for luxury, exquisite pleasure, being pampered. It appears again in Isaiah 55:2 — *"delight yourselves in rich food"* — and Isaiah 58:14 — *"you will take delight in the Lord."* It is a word of **appetite and enjoyment**, not obligation.

---

#### Jesus as the Perfect Model of Delight

Jesus himself is the one who perfectly delights in the Father — and we learn to delight in God *through* Him.

- **Isaiah 42:1** — The Father declares: *"Here is my servant, whom I uphold, my chosen one in whom I delight."* The Father's delight rests on the Son, and the Son's whole life is oriented toward the Father's pleasure.
- **John 4:34** — Jesus says His *food* is to do the will of the One who sent Him. This is not reluctant obedience — this is delight expressed as sustenance. The Father's will is what He hungers for.
- **Luke 10:21** — Jesus *rejoices in the Holy Spirit* when speaking to the Father — a spontaneous, Spirit-filled joy that erupts from His relationship with the Father. This is what delight in God looks like in the Son's own life.
- **Psalm 16:11** — *"In your presence there is fullness of joy; at your right hand are pleasures forevermore."* Applied Messianically (Acts 2:25–28), this is Christ's own experience of the Father — the one at the Father's right hand who knows the fullness of His presence.

---

#### What Delight Is (and Is Not)

Delight in the Lord is fundamentally about **finding your deepest pleasure and satisfaction in God Himself** — in who He is, not merely what He does.

It is **not**:
- The same as being happy about your circumstances
- A reward earned through good behaviour
- A feeling manufactured through effort
- Simply enjoying worship music or Christian activities

Many people subtly replace delight *in God* with delight *in things about God* — theology, Bible knowledge, church community, answered prayer. These are good things but can become substitutes for the real thing.

It **is**:
1. **Knowing Him well enough to enjoy Him** — You cannot delight in someone you do not know. Delight grows through time in His presence, through Scripture that reveals His character, through prayer that builds familiarity. The Psalms are a school of this — writers who have *tasted* God and cannot stop talking about how good He is (Psalm 34:8, Psalm 63:1–5, Psalm 84:1–2).
2. **Finding Him beautiful** — Psalm 27:4 speaks of *"one thing"* — to gaze upon the *beauty* of the Lord. This is an aesthetic and relational response to who God is. His holiness, steadfast love, wisdom, and faithfulness — when these become *wonderful* rather than just doctrinally correct, delight begins to form.
3. **Preferring His presence over substitutes** — Psalm 73:25: *"Whom have I in heaven but you? And there is nothing on earth I desire besides you."* Asaph arrived here after a long struggle. Delight is often the other side of discovering that everything else ultimately disappoints.
4. **Joy that is not dependent on circumstances** — This is what Paul demonstrates in Philippians: in prison, facing possible execution, yet rejoicing — not because prison is pleasant, but because his delight is anchored in something prison cannot touch.

---

#### The Promise of Psalm 37:4

*"Delight yourself in the Lord, and He will give you the desires of your heart."*

This is often misread as a transaction: delight in God → get what you want. The deeper reading is that **when you genuinely delight in God, your desires are transformed**. What you want begins to align with what He wants. The promise is not wish-fulfilment — it is the miracle of having your heart reshaped so that what He gives *is* what you desire.

---

#### How Delight Grows

Delight is not commanded into existence — it is **cultivated**. It grows through:

- Unhurried time in the Psalms, which are themselves a school of delight
- Prayerful meditation on God's character and past faithfulness
- Gratitude — deliberately naming specific things about God that are wonderful
- Honest prayer that brings your real self to God, not a performed version
- Obedience — Jesus says in John 15 that keeping His commands leads to His joy being *in* us and our joy being *complete*

Augustine captures the heart of it: *"You have made us for yourself, O Lord, and our heart is restless until it rests in you."* Delight in the Lord is ultimately the heart coming to rest in the One it was always made for — and in Jesus, that rest is made possible and is perfectly modelled.

## Priority Books at a Glance

  

If you want to know where to anchor yourself, these are the books that carry the most weight across the entire study:

The Gospels — Matthew, Mark, Luke, John (your foundation — read all four) Isaiah — especially chapters 7, 9, 11, 40, 52–53 (the OT prophet who sees Jesus most clearly) Psalms — especially 2, 16, 22, 45, 110 (prophetic and typological) Romans — the theological engine of the cross and resurrection Hebrews — the single best book for understanding Jesus as priest, sacrifice, and fulfillment of the OT Colossians — His cosmic identity and supremacy Philippians 2 — His humility and exaltation Revelation 1 and 19–22 — the exalted, reigning, returning King
