
# Flesh

### 1. The Literal Meaning — Physical Body/Skin

At its most basic, _flesh_ simply means physical matter. When John says _"the Word became flesh"_ (John 1:14), he means Jesus took on a real human body. When Genesis says _"they shall become one flesh"_ (2:24), it refers to the physical union of marriage. Nothing negative here — just the material reality of being human.

The Hebrew word is **בָּשָׂר** (_basar_) and the Greek is **σάρξ** (_sarx_). Both can carry this neutral, physical meaning.

---

### 2. The Pauline Meaning — The Fallen Human Nature

This is where it gets theologically rich and is probably what you're asking about.

Paul uses _sarx_ (flesh) in a specialised way — particularly in Romans, Galatians, and Ephesians — to describe something deeper than just the physical body. He uses it to mean **the whole of fallen human nature apart from God.** It's the self that is oriented _inward and away from God_ by default — the ego, the pride, the self-sufficiency, the instinct to live independently of God.

Key distinction: **it's not the body that is evil.** Paul is not saying physical matter is bad (that would be Gnosticism, which the New Testament firmly rejects). He is saying that there is a _disposition_ within fallen humanity — a bent toward self, sin, and independence from God — and he calls that _the flesh_.

Romans 8:5–8 is one of the clearest summaries:

> _"Those who live according to the flesh set their minds on the things of the flesh, but those who live according to the Spirit set their minds on the things of the Spirit... the mind set on the flesh is death... the mind set on the flesh is hostile to God."_

Notice — it's about what the **mind is set on**, not just what the body does.

---

### 3. The Works of the Flesh — Galatians 5

Paul lists the _"works of the flesh"_ in Galatians 5:19–21 and they are revealing — because they include not just sexual sin and drunkenness, but also **idolatry, jealousy, rivalry, selfish ambition, and divisions.** These are not physical acts — they are attitudes and orientations of the heart. This confirms that _flesh_ for Paul is not primarily about bodily appetites but about the self-centred, God-excluding disposition of fallen humanity.

---

### 4. The Flesh vs The Spirit

Paul sets up a contrast throughout Romans 7–8 and Galatians 5:

- **The flesh** pulls toward self, sin, independence from God, death
- **The Spirit** pulls toward God, others, transformation, life

The Christian life is described as a real ongoing conflict between these two. Galatians 5:17 — _"the flesh desires what is contrary to the Spirit, and the Spirit what is contrary to the flesh. They are in conflict with each other."_

Crucially, Paul says in Romans 8:13 — _"if you live according to the flesh you will die, but if by the Spirit you put to death the deeds of the body, you will live."_ This is not passive. It's active, ongoing, and requires the Spirit's power.

---

### 5. The Flesh and the Cross

One of Paul's most striking statements is Galatians 2:20 — _"I have been crucified with Christ. It is no longer I who live, but Christ who lives in me."_ And Galatians 5:24 — _"Those who belong to Christ Jesus have crucified the flesh with its passions and desires."_

The cross is where the flesh is dealt with. Not eradicated in this life — Christians still feel its pull — but decisively defeated and no longer ruling. The believer is no longer _enslaved_ to it.

---

### The Short Summary

|Usage|Meaning|
|---|---|
|Literal|Physical body or human beings generally|
|Pauline|Fallen human nature — the self apart from God|
|In contrast to Spirit|The inward pull toward sin, self, and independence from God|

The flesh is not your body being evil. It is the _orientation_ of your whole self — body, mind, will — when it is turned away from God and curved in on itself. That is why delight in the Lord is actually the _antidote_ to the flesh — because delight reorients desire back toward God rather than toward self.


**Penal Substitution** (your "penilsubstitution")

This is the dominant Western, especially Reformed/evangelical, view of the atonement. The idea is that God's justice required punishment for sin, and Jesus took that punishment _in our place_ — a legal transaction. He bore the _penalty_ we deserved. This is the framework most Western evangelicals grew up with: "Jesus paid the fine."

---

**Recapitulation**

This is a much older view, going back to **Irenaeus** (2nd century). The word literally means "to sum up again" or "to redo from the head." The idea is that Adam failed humanity at every stage — Jesus came and _relived the whole human story_, but got it right. He recapitulated (redid, summed up) everything Adam undid. Salvation is less about a legal transaction and more about Jesus _restoring and completing humanity itself_.

---

**Theosis** (also called _deification_ or _divinization_)

This is a predominantly **Eastern Orthodox** concept. The idea, rooted in 2 Peter 1:4 ("partakers of the divine nature"), is that the goal of salvation isn't just forgiveness — it's _union with God_, being transformed into His likeness, sharing in His very life and nature. Athanasius famously said: _"God became man so that man might become God"_ — not meaning we become divine beings, but that we participate in the divine life.
